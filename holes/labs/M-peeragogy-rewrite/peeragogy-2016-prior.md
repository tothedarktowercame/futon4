# Why did peeragogy matter in 2016? — the rubric (a prior)

**Date drafted:** 2026-04-29
**Mission:** [M-peeragogy-rewrite](../../missions/M-peeragogy-rewrite.md), MAP phase, inward step
**Sources:** Peeragogy Handbook V1.0, live wikitext at
`futon4/data/essays/wikibooks/peeragogy-handbook/wikitext/`. Specific
chapters cited per claim.

## What this is

This document extracts what the Peeragogy Handbook says *about itself*
into a checkable rubric of claims. Following the Pragmatist inquiry
chain set out in the mission's IDENTIFY (§1, *why does peeragogy → what
is peeragogy → why do these patterns matter and matter-together*), the
rubric is the **2016 prior**: the inside view of peeragogy in its own
voice, distilled from the Handbook's reflexive moments (preface,
foreword, revised intro, how-to-use, peeragogical assessment chapter,
antipatterns chapter, peeragogy in action chapter).

The 2026 update — the **posterior** — comes from MAP's outward survey
applying this rubric to other surfaces (podcast transcripts, adjacent
literatures, current 2026 collectives where the patterns recur named or
unnamed). For each claim below, a placeholder `### 2026 update:` line
marks where the posterior pass writes back; the prior itself stays
unchanged so the update is visible.

The rubric is organised around five questions the Pragmatist chain
asks of any practice tradition:

1. **Why does it?** — what gap does the practice address?
2. **What is it?** — positive definitional claims
3. **What does it care about?** — commitments / values
4. **What is it not?** — boundary claims (primarily via the antipatterns)
5. **What about its own form?** — reflexive claims about how the practice represents itself

A sixth section catalogs the patterns and antipatterns themselves, since
these are the operational content the Handbook claims constitutes
peeragogy's named moves.

## Living rubric — discipline added 2026-04-29

The rubric is *not* applied as a frozen reference. As the posterior pass
walks through external surfaces (podcast transcripts, adjacent
literatures, current 2026 collectives), evidence will surface claims and
questions the original prior didn't contain. The discipline:

- **One new rubric item per episode (or external source).** A
  *substantive* addition surfaced by the source gets elevated from the
  per-episode posterior notes into the rubric proper, becoming a
  first-class item the rest of the corpus is then tested against.
  Multiple per-episode additions stay in posterior notes; only the
  highest-leverage one per source is promoted.
- **Provenance is preserved.** Each promoted item is marked
  `**Added 2026-MM-DD from <source> posterior pass.**` so the
  prior/posterior distinction stays traceable.
- **Original prior items are immutable.** They reflect the 2016
  Handbook's self-understanding; they don't get rewritten as evidence
  arrives. Their `### 2026 update:` lines hold the posterior write-back
  in one place; the new items added per the discipline above hold
  *additions* the original Handbook didn't make.
- **Promotion criteria.** An addition is worth promoting if (a) it
  changes the carrier-shape decision, (b) it extends a bottleneck or a
  framing the prior commits to, (c) it names a pattern or move the
  prior misses entirely, or (d) at least one other source has independently
  echoed the same point. (b)+(c) are the most common.

Post-2016 additions live in `## Post-2016 additions` subsections at the
end of each section.

Now, with access to Open Educational Resources and free or inexpensive communication platforms, groups of people can learn together outside as well as inside formal institutions."

**1.2 Self-learners outside formal institutions lack lore for learning together without a teacher.** *Foreword (Rheingold):* "What's missing for learners outside formal institutions who know how to use social media is useful lore about how people learn together without a teacher."

> ### 2026 update: ___

**1.3 The methods, not the technologies, are the next step.** *Foreword:* "I think that we're beginning to see the next step, which is to develop the methods — we certainly have the technologies, accessible at the cost of broadband access — for self-learners to teach and learn from each other more effectively." *Operative claim:* technologies are no longer the bottleneck; methods are.

> ### 2026 update: ___

**1.4 The organising question.** *Foreword:* "How does a group of self-learners organize co-learning?" — the Handbook's central operational question.

> ### 2026 update: ___

(2) "How can this group identify and select the best learning resources about that topic?" (3) "How will these learners identify and select the appropriate technology and communications tools and platforms to accomplish their learning goal?" (4) "What does the group need to know about learning theory and practice to put together a successful peer-learning program?"

> ### 2026 update: ___

**1.6 Peer production has built Wikipedia and GNU/Linux but hasn't yet demonstrated its power in education.** *Peeragogy in Action:* "It is clear to us that the techniques of peer production that have built and continue to improve *Wikipedia* and GNU/Linux have yet to fully demonstrate their power in education. We believe that the *Peeragogy Handbook* can help change that..."

### Post-2016 additions to section 1

**1.8 Ethics is the next bottleneck after methods.** **Added 2026-04-29 from ep 2 posterior pass (Lane Raspberry, Wikimedian-in-Residence at U. Virginia).** *"Don't worry about the technology, the info, the analysis, all this work. Those things will get sorted out. What I'm not sure is gonna get sorted out is the right ethical foundation for all of this. That's where we really need help."* Extends the rubric's bottleneck-hierarchy: 2014–2016 said tech → methods; 2026 says tech → methods → ethics. Significant for the carrier-shape decision because ethics-as-bottleneck pulls the critical-frame articulation from one frame among five to a load-bearing concern any carrier must carry.

> ### 2026 update: ___

**1.9 Pre-existing community is the upstream variable; course-creation (or carrier-creation more generally) without an existing community to serve produces abandoned artifacts, not learning.** **Added 2026-04-29 from ep 12 posterior pass (Peter Shukie, COOPS / Community Open Online Courses, ~10 years of evidence).** *"What was missing was how do you create a community online? How do you go and find those other people? They don't just happen and stumble across. It's not a high street where the people in it go past that building. It's a website, it's a web address. There's absolutely nothing that there is no way you would encounter this unless somebody comes and finds you."* Peter's COOPS project — designed as a third option between cMOOC and xMOOC, deliberately small and local — discovered over 10 years that individual course-creation in hope of attracting strangers does not work. The shift was to *"work with organizations and help them create the COOPS"* — pre-existing community first, course second.

The 2016 Handbook implicitly assumes the audience exists ("a group of people who want to learn together can"); it doesn't name *finding the group* as the upstream task. **Significant for the carrier-shape decision in DERIVE.** Any carrier (zine series, accelerator, course, infrastructure) presupposes a community-or-network it's serving. The carrier is downstream of the community, not the other way around. Bears on rubric items 3.1 (polycentric leadership), 3.9 (stewardship-succession), 3.10 (reciprocal-participation), and 5.7 (co-revery) — all of which assume a community is present. The rest of the corpus gets tested for whether speakers exhibit pre-existing-community-awareness, treat the audience as auto-arriving, or surface the community-finding work explicitly.

## 2. What is peeragogy? (Positive definitional claims)

**2.1 A field of study.** *Revised Intro:* "Peeragogy is a field of study for collaborative learning and collaborative working."

> ### 2026 update: ___

**2.2 A peer-produced and peer-facilitated approach.** (External, per Joe's `corneli2026patterns` paper §sec:peeragogy — included here because the same definition appears in the Handbook's published companion essays.) "Peeragogy is a peer-produced and peer-facilitated approach to learning in which participants collaboratively develop goals, roles, and artifacts, and iteratively refine their practices through reflection and coordination."

> ### 2026 update: ___

**2.3 Both a theory of learning and an operational method.** Same source: "peeragogy is both a theory of learning and an operational method for organizing shared inquiry and production." *Implication:* the practice and the theory are not separable.

> ### 2026 update: ___

**2.4 A riff on pedagogy with explicit genealogy.** *Overview:* "our new term, peeragogy, is a riff on the word pedagogy — the art, science, or profession of teaching. Pedagogy has a somewhat problematic story of origin: it comes from the ancient Greek tradition of having a child (paidos) be supervised (agogos) by a slave." Self-positions downstream of and against the pedagogy/andragogy lineage.

> ### 2026 update: ___

**2.5 A practical guide to online co-learning.** *How to use this Handbook (Rheingold):* "This Wikibook edition of the Peeragogy Handbook is a practical guide to online co-learning, a living document..." *Note:* "online" is load-bearing here — peeragogy as articulated in the Handbook is specifically online-mediated co-learning.

> ### 2026 update: ___

**2.6 Self-organize peer learning.** *How to use this Handbook:* "If you and a group of other people want to use digital media and networks to co-learn together, this Wikibook is a practical tool for learning how to self-organize peer learning — what we call 'peeragogy.'"

> ### 2026 update: ___

### Post-2016 additions to section 2

**2.7 Peeragogy names something pre-existing rather than something newly built.** **Added 2026-04-29 from ep 1 posterior pass (Charles Jeffrey Danoff).** *"It's also … where when people were figuring out how to make fire for the first time, they were producing fire, they're learning from one another. So just a way to describe what humans have been doing for a very long time. … We didn't discover PureGadji [Peeragogy]. It's just are all around."* The 2016 prior frames peeragogy as a 2014–2016 method-development project; ep 1 reframes it as a vocabulary for a pre-existing practice. The two framings can coexist but they make different appeals to attention: the build-framing recruits collaborators to a project; the timeless-framing recruits noticing to a practice. Bears directly on the practice-supporting-doctrine analogy from the mission's IDENTIFY (sangha and consciousness-raising were not invented when they were named).

> ### 2026 update: ___

**2.8 Peeragogy is a worldview/metaphor shift (from "professor knows best" to "we all create together"), not a method or curriculum.  The shift operates at CLA layers 3-4 (worldview and myth), not just at layers 1-2 (litany and systemic).** **Added 2026-04-29 from ep 13 posterior pass (Sohail Inayatullah, futures-studies practitioner / Metafuture co-founder, performing outside-voice CLA-analysis of peeragogy itself).** *"The old world was the lecture. The worldview is pedagogy, the metaphor's professor knows best. You've shifted it to peeragogy, meaning we all create together. And instead of the lecture, it's now peer to peer."* This is the most substantive outside-voice naming of what peeragogy IS across the entire podcast corpus — from a futures-studies practitioner who applies CLA to ministries / Interpol / UNESCO worldwide, recognizing peeragogy as a worldview/metaphor shift in his own framework's terms.

**Significant for the carrier-shape decision in DERIVE.** A method-carrier would only operate at CLA layers 1-2 (litany — what to do, and systemic — why it works).  A metaphor/worldview-carrier needs to work at layers 3-4 (worldview — what kind of relationship is presupposed, and myth — what story makes the practice make sense).  Suggests carriers (b)/(e) — zines or practice-supporting infrastructure that explicitly address worldview and metaphor — over carrier (a) — chapter rewrite that inherits the Handbook's mostly-layer-2 framing.  Pairs with rubric item 2.7 (peeragogy names something pre-existing): the practice predates the name because the worldview/metaphor shift it names predates any specific methodology that operationalizes it.  Sangha and consciousness-raising are direct analogues — both name worldview/metaphor shifts that predate their specific methodological elaborations.

### Post-2016 additions to section 3

**3.9 Stewardship-succession is the unwritten requirement of polycentric practices.** **Added 2026-04-29 from ep 4 posterior pass (blackbox-recorder framing — Howard Rheingold + Joe Corneli + Bryan Alexander).** The rubric's polycentric-leadership claim (3.1) assumes succession will happen organically when the convener steps back. Ep 4 is six-years-later evidence that it doesn't, for the canonical-text production task: Howard convened peeragogy at Berkeley in 2012, scaffolded the project, eventually stepped back; Joe in 2020 wanted v4 of the Handbook and had drafted v5; ten years after the 2016 third edition, neither v4 nor v5 has shipped. The practice continued (podcasts, Cool Laboratory, Future Trends Forum, Metacog reading group) but the canonical artifact stalled. Polycentric practices that don't have explicit succession of the convener role drift toward a steady state — they continue at reduced scope but don't ship next-canonical-things. The rest of the corpus gets tested for whether speakers exhibit, name, or work around this stewardship-succession gap.

> ### 2026 update: ___

**3.10 Reciprocal participation: extracting from a community requires giving back to that community.** **Added 2026-04-29 from ep 5 posterior pass (Paola Ricaurte + Yasmin Bidyoff, both articulated independently).** Paola: *"if we extract something from the community, we should give back that as well."* Yasmin: *"a reciprocal participation, social contract sort of thing."* The Handbook's chapter 17 (Peeragogical Assessment) hints at reciprocity through its "learning is adaptation" gloss but doesn't formalise reciprocal-participation as a first-class discipline. The 2026 voice — informed by feminist data frameworks, indigenous data frameworks, and Design Justice — names it directly. Significant for the newly-promoted item 1.8: if ethics is the bottleneck, reciprocal-participation is one of the concrete ethics-disciplines a carrier can embody. The rest of the corpus gets tested for whether speakers exhibit reciprocal participation as a stance, name it as a missing discipline, or treat the give-back move as discretionary.

> ### 2026 update: ___

**3.11 The practice's enabling conditions (volunteer energy, subsidised infrastructure, institutional anchoring) should be visible, not assumed.** **Added 2026-04-29 from ep 8 posterior pass (James + Jeff Monroe, ACMI / Arlington Community Media).** *"because we are not having to spend time fundraising, seeking sponsorship, or at least not this kind have we had to, because we are funded effectively and to great benefit to our community... reluctantly by them, and so we happen to have here in Arlington, three cable companies operating RCN, Comcast, and Verizon, and we, you know, they have to get licenses from the town, negotiate those licenses... the town... will extract a commitment from the cable companies to set aside some portion of their gargantuan profits for the operations of an ACMI."* The 2016 Handbook implicitly assumes peer learning sustains itself on volunteer energy. ACMI shows what becomes possible when the practice has a structural funding source (cable franchise fees, in this case); other long-running practices have other enabling conditions (institutional anchoring, grant cycles, volunteer-time aggregation, paid stewardship). The discipline: name the enabling conditions explicitly, recognise their fragility (James also names that political shifts can erode the funding model), and design carriers that match what's actually available rather than what would be ideal. Bears on rubric item 3.9 (stewardship-succession): ACMI's *grow-your-own-stewards-from-membership* pattern depends on having paid staff slots to grow into, which in turn depends on subsidised infrastructure. The rest of the corpus gets tested for whether speakers name their practice's enabling conditions, treat them as fragile, or assume they will simply continue.

> ### 2026 update: ___

**3.12 Diversity-of-the-group, not expertise of the participants, is the operative variable for collective intelligence in peer learning.** **Added 2026-04-29 from ep 9 posterior pass (Howard Rheingold, citing collective-intelligence research).** *"The recent work on collective intelligence has shown that it's not the particular expertise of the people in a group that contributes to its collective intelligence. It's really the diversity of the group. So we need people who want to learn together. Whether or not you have any expertise at all."* Direct counter to expert-centric framings that assume peer learning works because of accumulated expertise in the room. Gives existing rubric items 3.1 (polycentric leadership), 6.0a (curating-not-experting), and 2.7 (timeless-practice — peeragogy is everywhere) a *research-grounded mechanism*: the value isn't in the expertise pooled but in the diversity composed. Bears on the carrier-shape decision — any carrier that recruits "by-credential" misses the operative variable; carriers that recruit "by-curiosity-and-difference" align with the mechanism. The rest of the corpus gets tested for whether speakers exhibit diversity-as-anchor, treat expertise as a precondition, or surface the tension between the two.

## 4. What does peeragogy say it isn't? (Boundary claims)

**4.1 Not a replacement for great teachers.** *Foreword:* "Nobody should ever overlook the fact that there are great teachers. Teachers should be trained, rewarded, and sought out. But it's time to expand the focus on learners, particularly on self-learners whose hunger for learning hasn't been schooled out of them." *Boundary:* peeragogy expands focus to self-learners; does not displace teachers.

> ### 2026 update: ___

**4.2 Not a normal book.** *Revised Intro:* "The Peeragogy Handbook isn't a normal book, it is an example of the kind of work that's only just possible now. It is an evolving guide, and it tells a collaboratively written story: it's one that you can help write."

> ### 2026 update: ___

**4.3 Not the last word.** *Foreword:* "We know that this isn't the last word. We hope it's a start. We invite new generations of editors, educators, learners, media-makers, web-makers, and translators to build on our foundation." *Boundary:* explicitly provisional; succession is invited.

> ### 2026 update: ___

**4.4 Boundary by negation — the antipatterns.** Chapter 22 names seven failure modes peeragogy distances itself from. Each antipattern is, by negation, a thing peeragogy says it is not, or a tendency it warns against:

- **Isolation** — peeragogy is not Not-Invented-Here insularity; effort that isolates itself "will not have the occasion to draw on other resources."
- **Magical Thinking** — peeragogy is not the assumption that the right platform / tool / structure will solve coordination magically. *"Magical thinking is the thief of process."*
- **Messy with Lurkers** — peeragogy acknowledges low-completion / high-lurking dynamics rather than mistaking them for failure. *"Co-learning is messy. It needs time, patience, confusion, re-forming, re-norming, re-storming."*
- **Misunderstanding Power Laws** — peeragogy is not surprised by 90/9/1 distributions; it works with them.
- **Navel-Gazing** — peeragogy is not reflection-without-action-surface; feedback must close into action.
- **Stasis** — peeragogy is not stuck-process; it has a Find-the-Moving-Dimension move when the project plateaus.
- **Weak-Tie Conflation** — peeragogy is not "everyone you can reach is a co-learner"; weak ties and strong ties are different and the conversion is structured (see chapter 26).

> ### 2026 update (per antipattern): ___

### Post-2016 additions to section 4

**4.5 Named patterns are not categorically good; the practice's relationship to its own pattern vocabulary must remain critical.** **Added 2026-04-29 from ep 6 posterior pass (Joe Corneli).** *"We shouldn't necessarily look at patterns only as things that we, as we like, we can get patterns of abuse or patterns of negative thinking, all kinds of things come in patterns."*  The Handbook's chapter 21 (patterns) and chapter 22 (antipatterns) implicitly classify: peeragogy patterns are good, antipatterns are bad. Ep 6 names a more critical relationship: pattern-recognition itself is morally neutral; the practice's job is to be critical of which patterns it adopts and which it avoids. The rubric's section 4 currently makes boundary claims by negation (the antipatterns); this addition makes a more general claim — that the *positive* pattern set is not above critique, and that the practice's reflexive layer must include critical pattern-evaluation, not just pattern-application. The rest of the corpus gets tested for whether speakers exhibit critical pattern-evaluation, name harmful patterns the practice could fall into, or treat the named patterns as defaults to adopt.

**5.1 The Handbook is a living document, not a static text.** *How to use this Handbook:* "a living document that invites comment and invites readers to join the community of editors; the Handbook does not have to be read in linear order from beginning to end."

> ### 2026 update: ___

**5.2 The Handbook documents the project documenting itself.** *Peeragogy in Action* IS the chapter that empirically documents what worked while writing the rest. The Handbook is partly a record of its own production.

> ### 2026 update: ___

**5.3 Polycentric authorship is exhibited, not just claimed.** Multiple voices across chapters; named author bylines per chapter; the Foreword by Rheingold names rotating contributors. The form-claim ("peer-produced") is meant to be visible in the structure.

> ### 2026 update: ___

**5.5 Provisional, succession-inviting voice throughout.** Phrases like "we hope," "this isn't the last word," "you can help write" recur across the reflexive chapters. *Form-implication:* the Handbook adopts a different rhetorical posture than a closed authoritative reference.

> ### 2026 update: ___

**5.6 Tonal commitment to play, humour, banter.** *Overview:* "Certainly some of our best learning moments in the Peeragogy project have been peppered with humor and banter." *Form-implication:* the prose itself is supposed to embody this; the antipattern *Stasis* is partly a tonal claim about what stuck process looks like and how to unstick it.

> ### 2026 update: ___

### Post-2016 additions to section 5

**5.7 The practice has a recharge / co-revery register: gatherings whose value is in the gathering itself, not in the artifacts they produce.** **Added 2026-04-29 from ep 7 posterior pass (Pete Kaminski, Open Global Mind).** *"I've described them as kind of a co-revery. Jerry's got a real gentle and effective way of kind of bringing people out and supporting them in conversation… It feels also like church, kind of like a Quaker meeting maybe. It's something where I've also called it dream energy. It's a recharging time for all of us to come together."* The Handbook's reflexive chapters describe the practice mostly in task-coordination terms (chapters 5 *Convening*, 9 *Adding Structure*, 14 *Co-Facilitation*). The Quaker-meeting analogy is direct evidence for IDENTIFY's practice-supporting-doctrine analogy: practices that survive without canonical texts (sangha, Quaker meeting, consciousness-raising circles) sustain themselves through gatherings whose value is the gathering itself. Significant for the carrier-shape decision — favors carrier (e) where the practice's primary surface is the recurring gathering. The rest of the corpus gets tested for whether speakers exhibit, name, or work around the recharge / co-revery register.

## 6. Operational content — the patterns and antipatterns

The Handbook's operational claim is that 12 process-patterns and 7
antipatterns name the moves that actually matter. The 2016 prior
includes these as named entities; whether the names are still apt and
whether the set is complete is a 2026-update question.

### Post-2016 additions to section 6

**6.0a Curating-not-experting (operational stance candidate).** **Added 2026-04-29 from ep 3 posterior pass (Charles Blass, Kiko Lab).** *"We're not experts, we're curators and we're bringing a lot of people into conversation and sort of connective intelligence in terms of the conversation as the foundation for what we do."* The position relative to expertise is itself a peeragogical move. Distinct from the Roles pattern (which is about *which* roles people take); this is about *what stance the role takes toward expertise* — not flat (no experts allowed) and not hierarchical (experts above), but curatorial (bringing voices into conversation). Candidate flexiarg in `futon3/library/peeragogy/`; for the rubric, the stance becomes a thing the rest of the corpus is tested against — does each subsequent transcript exhibit the curating-not-experting disposition, or does it slide into expert-talking-down or peer-flat-without-stewardship?

> ### 2026 update: ___

**6.0b Calling-in (not calling-out): an operational stance for disagreement-friendly facilitation, specifically when someone has said something the group might want to push back against.** **Added 2026-04-29 from ep 10 posterior pass (Richard Butler, DEI consultant).** *"I never call them out, always call them in immediately at that point. If it's something that could create, that could cause a person or a group to ball up their fists and want to fight, then I like to call them in a little later. So not to embarrass them, not to have them be defensive, but to weave it in."* The Handbook's chapter 14 (Co-Facilitation) discusses moderation generally; the *call-in not call-out* framing is a more specific operational stance about how to invite engagement when someone has said something the group might want to push back against. Concrete in-voice example: Richard's 550-person diversity workshop where a participant pushed back against Black Lives Matter framing — Richard responded *"I'm going to agree with you that all lives matter, but can we agree that not all lives are equal?"* — calling-in operationalised under live conditions.  Adjacent to but distinct from 6.0a (curating-not-experting): curating is about facilitator's stance toward expertise; calling-in is about facilitator's stance toward disagreement.  The pair work together as a disagreement-friendly facilitation stance.  Candidate flexiarg in `futon3/library/peeragogy/` (paired with `brave-safe-space`).

> ### 2026 update: ___

**6.0c Check-in at the start of meetings: an operational discipline of inviting each participant to name where they are at mentally before beginning the agenda.** **Added 2026-04-29 from ep 11 posterior pass (Leo Vivier, EmacsConf organizer).** *"Sticking to a particular mode of debriefing when we do something which is called checking in with people, where make sure that people say where they are at mentally. And then we move on to usually an agenda working on different points."* The newly-flexiarg'd `peeragogy/par` closes meetings; this opens them. Together they bracket the meeting form. The Handbook's chapter 14 (Co-Facilitation) discusses moderation generally but doesn't name a specific opening discipline. Candidate flexiarg in `futon3/library/peeragogy/` (paired with `peeragogy/par` — the bookend pair). The rest of the corpus gets tested for whether speakers name an opening discipline distinct from the agenda, treat the start of meetings as functional preamble only, or surface the cost of skipping.

# Synthesis — 2016 prior vs 2026 podcast posterior

**Date:** 2026-04-29
**Mission:** [M-peeragogy-rewrite](../../../missions/M-peeragogy-rewrite.md), MAP phase
**Sources:** [The 2016 prior rubric](../../peeragogy-2016-prior.md) (60 immutable items + 13 post-2016 additions promoted across the pass) + 13 per-episode posterior notes in `posterior-notes/01-...md` through `13-...md` + 65 annotations in the podcast Essays catalog at `data/essays/podcasts/peeragogy-podcasts/peeragogy-podcasts-book-annotations.el`.

This document compares the 2016 Handbook's self-understanding (the
prior rubric) with the 2026 podcast voice (the posterior pass), and
draws out what shifted, what corroborated, what got challenged, and
what's now visible as candidate material for ARGUE and the
carrier-shape decision in DERIVE.

It is a MAP-stage artifact. **It is not yet ARGUE.**  Findings
synthesised; the 2026 *why peeragogy matters* answer is not written
here.

## What was on file as the 2016 prior

The rubric (`peeragogy-2016-prior.md`) extracts the Handbook's
self-claims into 60 items across 7 sections:

1. **Why does peeragogy?** (7 items): the gap peeragogy fills — school monopoly broken, missing lore, methods-not-tech bottleneck, the organising question, four mindful questions, peer-production-hasn't-yet-hit-education, could-change-educational-institutions.
2. **What is peeragogy?** (6 items): positive definitional claims — field of study, peer-produced/peer-facilitated, theory + operational method, riff on pedagogy, online co-learning, self-organize peer learning.
3. **What does peeragogy care about?** (8 items): commitments / values — polycentric leadership, open licensing, self-assessment, motivation/play, learning-as-adaptation, self-application as the test, bridge to wider OER ecosystem, distributed contribution.
4. **What does peeragogy say it isn't?** (4 main + 7 antipattern items): boundary claims — not-a-replacement-for-teachers, not-a-normal-book, not-the-last-word, plus the 7 antipatterns from chapter 22 as boundary claims.
5. **What about its own form?** (6 items): reflexivity — living document, documents-itself-documenting, polycentric authorship exhibited, self-application performed, succession-inviting voice, tonal commitment to play.
6. **Operational content** (12 process patterns + 7 antipatterns from chapters 21+22).
7. **What would update this prior?** (the question list MAP's outward survey is testing against).

## What corroborated

Some prior items got strong cross-episode corroboration.  These are
load-bearing in 2026 voice across multiple speakers and contexts:

- **2.7 (timeless-practice / peeragogy names something pre-existing)**: 7+ corroborations across the corpus.
  - Ep 1 (Charlie Danoff): *"we didn't discover PureGadji"*
  - Ep 4 (Charlotte): *"as we tell everyone, Pyrogogy is everywhere. We didn't discover it"*
  - Ep 5 (Joe): Mexican femicide-resistance work — *"they don't call it PuroGudgy"*
  - Ep 7 (Pete Kaminski): OGM Thursday calls as *"co-revery... Quaker meeting"*
  - Ep 8 (Charlotte): ACMI as practice peeragogy *"digs out from under rocks in studios"*
  - Ep 9 (Howard + Charlie): *"a fancy word"* / *"we didn't invent peeragogy"*
  - Ep 11 (Leo): Emacs Workshops *"inherited from Linux user groups"*
  - Ep 12 (Peter Shukie): direct agreement: *"You said before about Piragaji's timeless, I agree"*
  - Ep 12 (David Preston): *techne* + stick-and-dirt-and-a-learner framing
  - Ep 13 (Sohail's CLA-analysis places peeragogy at worldview/myth layers — the practice the name points to predates any specific methodology)

  **Item 2.7 is the most load-bearing finding in the corpus.**  Multiple speakers, multiple contexts, convergent framing.  Bears directly on ARGUE and on the carrier-shape decision.

- **3.6 (self-application is the test)**: Confirmed across most episodes by the recurring PAR practice (eps 1-7, 10-13) and by the practice-applied-to-itself moves (Sohail's CLA-of-peeragogy in ep 13; Charles' wrapper-pattern-named-and-applied in ep 3; Howard's convener-stepping-back in ep 4 and 9).

- **5.5 (provisional, succession-inviting voice)**: Confirmed across most episodes.  The voice is consistently provisional — *"this is a pilot," "we don't have an idea right now of exactly how," "please bring us topics."*

- **3.7 (bridge to wider OER ecosystem)**: Strongly extended.  The corpus surfaced bridges to: Wikipedia / News on Wiki / Wikidata / WikiCite, Open Global Mind, Federated Wiki / Roam / OrgRoam / Kumu / Miro, Kiko Lab + Cool Laboratory, Future Trends Forum, ACMI community-media network, Cronkite School journalism, Afro Crowd, Tierra Común Network, A+ Alliance, no-tech-for-tyrants, Ada Lovelace Institute, Berkman Klein, Design Justice / indigenous data / feminist data frameworks, EmacsConf / Emacs Workshops / Linux User Groups, COOPS, Open Source Learning, Metafuture / CLA, anticipation studies, futures studies, Bryan Alexander's *Academia Next*, Mike Caulfield's misinformation work, Howard Rheingold's writing.  *Wide* network of adjacent practice that the Handbook's chapter 24 (Peeragogies Technology) doesn't gesture at.

## What got qualified or extended

Some prior items survived but with substantive qualification:

- **1.3 (methods, not tech, are the bottleneck)**: 2014-2016 said tech is no longer the bottleneck; methods are.  2026 evidence extends:
  - Ep 2 (Lane): tech → methods → ethics
  - Ep 7 (Pete Kaminski): tech → methods → ethics → information-format-interoperability
  - Ep 10 (Richard Butler): vulnerability-aversion is the structural blocker
  - Ep 12 (David Preston): *techne* sidelines tech-as-bottleneck entirely (stick + dirt + a learner)
  
  The 2016 framing is mostly intact but stops too soon.  2026 voice names a deeper layer.

- **2.5 (online co-learning specifically)**: 2016 framing was *online*-loaded.  2026 voice consistently challenges this:
  - Ep 1 (Joe): *"but it doesn't happen only on the digital environment"*
  - Ep 8 (ACMI): physical-studio-mediated peer learning at scale
  - Ep 12 (David): stick + dirt + a learner
  
  Online is one mode; not constitutive.

- **3.1 (polycentric leadership)**: Strongly confirmed but refined:
  - Ep 2 (Pete): Wikipedia is *decentralized on social axis, centralized on ontological axis*
  - Ep 7 (Pete Kaminski): OGM has *built-in two-person succession* from the start
  - Ep 8 (ACMI): *grow-your-own-stewards-from-membership* via paid-staff-with-volunteer-pipeline
  - Ep 11 (Leo): *locomotive metaphor* — kernel of regulars on cadence carries pulse-energy contributors

  The 2016 *polycentric* claim doesn't articulate which axes are centralised vs decentralised, doesn't name what minimum kernel sustains the practice, doesn't distinguish bolted-on vs built-in succession.  All of these are 2026 refinements.

- **3.12 (diversity-not-expertise)** *(newly added from ep 9)*: Refined by:
  - Ep 11 (Leo): *quantitative growth ≠ diversity growth*
  - Ep 13 (Ivana): *surface diversity vs structural diversity* — princess-book example shows 12 stories that LOOK diverse share identical structure

  *Which kind* of diversity matters is the substantive question; the bare claim is necessary but not sufficient.

- **4.4 (boundary by negation — antipatterns)**: Strongly confirmed.  Speakers in multiple episodes name antipatterns operationally (Yasmin's window-dressing-ethics in ep 5; Bryan's Tech-Lash and credentialism in ep 4; David's intellectual-sharecroppers in ep 12).  The 2026 critical frame from inside the practice is consistent.

## What got challenged

A few prior items got direct challenge:

- **5.1 (living document)**: The Handbook itself hasn't lived since 2016.  Ep 4 is the blackbox-recorder evidence: Joe wanted v4, drafted v5; six years later (now ten years), neither shipped.  Ep 6 and ep 9 confirm the wish-without-action.  The form-claim is honest about the 2016 Handbook's intent; the 2026 reality contradicts it for the canonical text.  *The practice has lived; the canonical-text-as-living-document claim has not.*

- **1.6 (peer production hasn't yet hit education)**: Strongly qualified.  Ep 2 (Wikipedia is peer production hitting knowledge-organisation since ~2001).  Ep 8 (ACMI runs free media-literacy programs in Arlington schools).  Ep 9 (Christy integrates Wikipedia editing into her Cronkite School course).  The 2016 framing reads as overcautious in retrospect; peer production HAS hit education, just at the local-classroom and civic scale rather than at the institution-wide scale the Handbook envisioned.

## What surfaced as new (and is now in the rubric)

The 13 promoted items name texture the Handbook lacks.  Three clusters:

**Cluster A — Stance-and-discipline triplet (section 6 additions):**
- 6.0a curating-not-experting (the facilitator's stance toward expertise)
- 6.0b calling-in not calling-out (the facilitator's stance toward disagreement)
- 6.0c check-in at meeting start (the discipline of opening meetings)

These three together name a coherent micro-vocabulary for facilitator practice.  The Handbook's chapter 14 (Co-Facilitation) discusses moderation generally but doesn't articulate this specific triplet.  Worth becoming a flexiarg cluster in `futon3/library/peeragogy/` (curating-not-experting and PAR are already mined; calling-in and check-in are next candidates).

**Cluster B — Practice-sustaining structure (section 3 additions):**
- 3.9 stewardship-succession (the convener's two-phase move needs phase 3: who picks up?)
- 3.10 reciprocal participation (extracting from a community requires giving back)
- 3.11 enabling conditions visible (volunteer energy / subsidised infrastructure / institutional anchoring should be named, not assumed)
- 3.12 diversity-not-expertise (operative variable for collective intelligence)

These four together name what sustains a polycentric practice across years.  The Handbook's chapter 21 names what the practice DOES (its operational moves); the cluster names what makes those moves continue to work.

**Cluster C — Framing-and-bottleneck reframes (sections 1, 2, 4, 5):**
- 1.8 ethics-as-bottleneck
- 1.9 pre-existing-community as upstream variable
- 2.7 peeragogy names something pre-existing (timeless-practice)
- 2.8 peeragogy is a worldview/metaphor shift, not a method or curriculum
- 4.5 patterns are not categorically good
- 5.7 co-revery / gathering-as-recharge register

These six reshape the Handbook's framing.  Items 2.7 and 2.8 together are the most consequential: peeragogy is a vocabulary for a pre-existing worldview/metaphor shift, not a methodology or curriculum.  This reframing affects nearly every downstream decision in the rewrite.

## What this means for the carrier-shape decision in DERIVE

The IDENTIFY-stage carrier shapes were:
- **(a)** Rewrite the existing Handbook chapter-by-chapter
- **(b)** Refactor the Handbook into a small boxed-set of mini-handbook zines (possibly one zine per pattern)
- **(c)** Hybrid — rewritten Handbook + extracted zine series
- **(d)** Some other text-shape that emerges from MAP
- **(e)** Practice-supporting infrastructure rather than a text
- **(f)** Teaching course that performs the practice while teaching it (added from ep 9)

**The corpus's findings argue against carrier (a) and toward carriers (b)/(e):**

- Item 2.8 (peeragogy is a worldview/metaphor shift, not a method): a chapter-rewrite carrier inherits the Handbook's mostly-layer-2 framing.  Carriers (b)/(e) can explicitly address worldview-and-myth.
- Item 2.7 + 1.9 together (peeragogy is timeless + pre-existing-community is upstream): the Handbook treats the audience as a thing to be reached; the 2026 voice treats the community as the upstream variable.  A chapter rewrite presupposes an audience; a zine-per-pattern or practice-supporting-infrastructure carrier serves an existing community more naturally.
- Item 3.9 + 3.11 (stewardship-succession + enabling conditions): the Handbook's 6-year stall (ep 4 blackbox) is direct evidence that the chapter-text production task failed for stewardship reasons.  A chapter-rewrite carrier inherits the same vulnerability.  Carrier (b) ships in pieces (each zine is a smaller production task); carrier (e) doesn't depend on canonical-text production at all.
- Item 5.7 (co-revery / gathering-as-recharge register): the Handbook doesn't have this register; carrier (e) is the most natural home for it.
- Item 6.0a + 6.0b + 6.0c (the stance-and-discipline triplet): these need to be embodied in operating practice, not just described.  Carrier (e) embodies; carrier (a) describes.

**Carrier (a) is not ruled out, but it carries more risk than the other shapes — and the risk is structural (it inherits the Handbook's stall mechanism), not just design-level.**

## What's now visible for ARGUE

The 2026 *why peeragogy matters* answer can draw on:

1. **Convergent practice across vocabularies.**  Ep 13's closing finding — Sohail's CLA-analysis recognising peeragogy as a worldview/metaphor shift, plus Ivana's mission-recognition — names that peeragogy is part of a wider family of convergent practice (CLA, futures studies, design justice, indigenous data frameworks, sangha, Quaker meeting, consciousness-raising).  Naming the family is itself useful work.

2. **The practice-supporting-doctrine analogy from IDENTIFY is well-corroborated.**  Multiple episodes name practices in the same family:
   - Ep 7: OGM Thursday calls as *co-revery / Quaker meeting / dream energy*
   - Ep 8: ACMI as *workplace-as-haven*
   - Ep 11: Emacs Workshops in the Linux-User-Groups lineage
   - Ep 13: CLA-of-the-self adjacent to mindfulness/contemplative-practice vocabulary
   
   The analogy isn't speculative; the corpus shows convergent shape.

3. **The minor-literature framing from IDENTIFY can be honest.**  The practice has 10 years of documented-but-not-canonical activity (the podcast itself).  Quantitative scholarly footprint is modest (708 GS hits vs ~20,200 for active inference).  But the operative variable (per item 3.12) is *diversity*, not *expertise* or *citation count*.  The minor-literature position can be claimed as a feature when the work names something the major literature can't see — and the corpus's evidence is that peeragogy names worldview/metaphor shifts that adjacent practices recognise from their own vocabularies.

4. **The ethics-as-bottleneck framing (1.8) gives ARGUE a deeper register.**  Lane Raspberry (ep 2): *"the right ethical foundation... that's where we really need help."*  David Preston (ep 12): *"the death we die inside before we stop living."*  Richard Butler (ep 10): vulnerability-aversion as structural blocker.  These are not policy concerns; they are operative mechanisms.  The 2026 *why peeragogy matters* answer can include that the practice serves the practitioner's relationship with their own integrity, not just an audience's learning needs.

5. **The "we didn't discover it" framing (2.7) re-positions the practice.**  Peeragogy's job in 2026 is less to *build a method* than to *name a thing already happening*, give that thing a vocabulary that practitioners can pick up selectively, and surface the convergent practice family across vocabularies.

6. **The Pragmatist inquiry chain from IDENTIFY (why does → what is → why do the patterns matter and matter-together)** holds up across the corpus.  Each link of the chain has substantial in-voice material now.

## Closing note on the rubric pass itself

The living-rubric discipline (one promotion per source) was a useful
constraint.  It prevented the rubric from ballooning while still
forcing each episode to leave a structural trace.  13 episodes →
13 additions → balanced distribution across all five framing-sections
plus the operational/stance section.  The other ~80 per-episode
additions (A1-A9 across episodes) stay in posterior notes as
secondary evidence.

The pass took a single working day across the multi-turn session.
~75,500 words of transcript synthesised against ~60 prior items into
13 promoted additions and 65 anchored annotations.  The artefacts
are queryable in the Essays browser (Peeragogy Podcast catalog with
13 sections; sidecar with 65 annotations) alongside the Handbook
catalog (31 sections; 77 annotations).  The infrastructure for joins
across the two catalogs is in place.

**Status:** MAP is now substantially populated for the

posterior is on file; the comparison is on file.  ARGUE and the

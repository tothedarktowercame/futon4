## Section 6 — Operational content

- **Newcomer pattern.** Yasmin is a new guest; Joe explicitly facilitates a low-touch introduction so the two guests can get to know each other. Newcomer-pattern as host-discipline rather than as participant-discipline.
- **Wrapper pattern (implicit).** Paola's *Data Epistemologies* paper is a Wrapper for the broader data-coloniality conversation.
- **6.0a Curating-not-experting** *(added ep 3)*. Yasmin's "elbowing in" framing is more activist than curatorial — *"we have to think creatively about elbowing our way into these conversations."* Distinct stance from curating, both can co-exist within the practice.
- **NEW pattern candidate: Reciprocal participation.** Paola: *"if we extract something from the community, we should give back that as well."* This is an ethics-of-data-collection move, distinct from existing patterns. Yasmin elaborates as a "reciprocal participation, social contract sort of thing." **Promoted to rubric (see below).**

## Section 7 — What would update this prior?

- **Recurrence in 2026 contexts.** Tierra Común Network, A+ Alliance, no-tech-for-tyrants, Mejente, Maria Salguero's femicide map, Ada Lovelace Institute. Concrete 2020s instances of peeragogy-adjacent practice (none called peeragogy by their practitioners).
- **Adjacent vocabularies.** *Data coloniality* (Paola). *Feminist data* / *indigenous data* / *Design Justice* (Paola). *AI justice* (Yasmin). *Body-as-first-territory* (Paola). *Reciprocal participation* (Yasmin). All adjacent to peeragogy but none in the Handbook's vocabulary.
- **Critical frame.** Strong evidence:
  - **Yasmin's reach question:** *"how are you working to incorporate meaningful public participation? Because most of the people that I collaborate with happen to be academics or technologists or industry people or artists and just like people who are already concerned and active. But what I'm really interested in is getting the people who are affected by this rapid technological development."*
  - **Joe's honest answer:** *"I would say Piragaji is often cited in the literature on MOOCs... I don't think we can say, oh yeah, we've nailed it... I think just engaging people in the process of writing is challenging."* Recognition from inside that peeragogy's reach hasn't crossed into communities outside the academic-technologist-artist-already-concerned set.
- **Practice-supporting-doctrine analogy.** Paola explicitly couples peeragogy to data-coloniality work; the practice survives the analogy by being *positioned alongside* feminist/indigenous/decolonial frameworks rather than as their replacement.
- **Form-claims tested.** PAR-off-broadcast continues (ep 5 is the third in a row to do this). The form-change from eps 1-2 (PAR in broadcast) → eps 3-5 (PAR in after-party) is a stable shift, not a one-off experiment.

## Additions to the prior (ep 5)

- **A1-ep5 (reciprocal participation / give-back-to-community as a discipline).** Paola: *"if we extract something from the community, we should give back that as well."* Yasmin: a *"reciprocal participation, social contract sort of thing."* Both speakers articulate the same ethics-of-data-collection move. The Handbook's chapter 17 (Peeragogical Assessment) hints at reciprocity ("learning is adaptation" applied socially) but doesn't formalise reciprocal-participation as a discipline. This is a section-3 (cares-about) addition. **Promoted to rubric (see below).**
- **A2-ep5 (body-as-first-territory framing).** Paola's territorial articulation of resistance: body → air → internet as nested territories. Distinct vocabulary the Handbook's chapters don't carry; bridges peeragogy to embodiment-aware practices.
- **A3-ep5 (build-products-to-make-a-point stance).** Yasmin: *"I'm very much a believer of building products to make a point, rather than endless reports."* Echoes A1-ep3 (peer-MAKING not just peer-learning) from a different speaker — corroboration of the making-side emphasis.
- **A4-ep5 (honest recognition of peeragogy's actual reach).** Joe's response to Yasmin's reach question is candid: peeragogy is mostly cited in MOOC literature; engagement beyond is challenging. This is critical-frame evidence FROM INSIDE the practice — peeragogy on its own terms acknowledges what it hasn't reached.
- **A5-ep5 (peeragogy-without-the-name in resistance work).** Joe's femicide-resistance example: practitioners who "don't call it PuroGudgy. They've never heard of us." Reconfirms timeless-practice claim 2.7 from a direct concrete example, not just a general framing.
- **A6-ep5 (Design Justice + indigenous-data + feminist-data as adjacent literatures).** Paola names three adjacent frameworks the rubric should test against. Each treats data ethics as a culture-specific discipline rather than a universal one.

## Promoted to rubric

**A1-ep5 (reciprocal participation / give-back-to-community as a discipline)** is the highest-leverage single addition; both guests articulate the same move from different vantage points (extracting-data ethics in Paola's case; researcher-participant social contract in Yasmin's case), and it directly fills out the newly-promoted item 1.8 (ethics is the bottleneck) with a concrete discipline the practice can adopt. Promoted as new rubric item **3.10 Reciprocal participation: extracting from a community requires giving back to that community**, per the living-rubric discipline.

## Section 6 — Operational content

- **PAR (Paragogical Action Review)** is the central operational artifact of ep 1 — *named explicitly, attributed historically* (Joe: *"Charlie and I originally came up with some variant of this in 2012"*; descended from US Army's After Action Review per Joe's later "meta comment"), *and applied live*. **This strongly evidences `C-mining-PAR` from the prior queue: PAR deserves elevation to a `peeragogy/` flexiarg.**
- **Newcomer pattern** — Charlotte's introductions of speakers + her gloss of "what is peeragogy" for new listeners (*"for anyone who's new to this whole thing"*) enact the Newcomer pattern.
- **Roles pattern** — producer (Charlotte), facilitator (Joe at start), guest-introducer (Charlotte), commentator (Charlie); roles named operationally.
- **Heartbeat pattern** — Joe: *"we've been doing these kinds of calls often, maybe not weekly, but pretty often for a long time."* Heartbeat as a long-running rhythm.
- **collaboration-coherence/messy-with-lurkers** (positive form) — Lisa's empathetic articulation of the discomfort of fluid org-structures, Charlie's response *"it's a lot harder to measure, and a lot messier … and that can be stressful and frustrating for, for many humans and understandably so."* The antipattern's positive form — naming the discomfort instead of pretending it isn't there — is exhibited.

## Section 7 — What would update this prior?

- **Recurrence in 2026 contexts.** Episode 1 names two:
  - **Scrum** (Joe: *"Pyrogagy is similar to in some ways Scrum"*) — adjacent vocabulary in software/business.
  - **StreamGuard's town halls** (Charlotte: *"They have a live town hall on Sunday nights … it's wildly successful because of that"*) — a 2026 corporate practice cited as enacting peeragogy.
  Outward MAP should follow both threads.
- **Adjacent vocabularies.** Scrum is an explicit cross-citation. After-Action Review is named as PAR's ancestor (US Army origin). These bridge to organisational-practice literatures the rubric should test.
- **Form-claims tested.** The podcast exhibits "living document" and "polycentric" form-claims **more strongly than the Handbook does in 2026** — the podcast continued past 2016; the Handbook's last edition stopped. Practice-supporting infrastructure outliving the canonical text is exactly what the practice-supporting-doctrine analogy (IDENTIFY anchor) predicts.
- **Critical frame.** Charlotte's tech-asymmetry observation ("*it does require a pretty sophisticated high powered internet connection*") is a concrete instance of the access-as-flattening critique. Honest engagement with this is in the room from episode 1.
- **Practice-supporting-doctrine analogy.** STRONGLY supported. The live PAR demo IS practice-as-text in real time. The podcast is a form of practice-supporting infrastructure that the Handbook only describes; it carries the practice without depending on the canonical text staying current.

## Additions to the prior (claims surfaced by ep 1, not in the rubric)

- **A1-ep1 (timeless-practice claim).** Charlie: *"what humans have been doing for a long time that maybe didn't term didn't exist … it's also appears where when people were figuring out how to make fire for the first time, they were producing fire, they're learning from one another … we didn't discover PureGadji. It's just are all around."* The Handbook frames peeragogy as a 2014–2016 method-development project; ep 1 reframes it as a name for something pre-existing. These two framings can coexist but they make different appeals to attention.
- **A2-ep1 (PAR as headline ritual).** The Handbook mentions PAR; the podcast elevates it to *the* operational signature. PAR is the move that lets the podcast self-document. Worth scoring as the most legible peeragogy move for newcomers.
- **A3-ep1 (concrete contrast with hierarchy).** Lisa's two-Japanese-automakers anecdote gives the Handbook's general "outside formal institutions" framing a concrete real-world contrast pair. Useful for any explainer text.
- **A4-ep1 (organisational scope).** Peeragogy applies to corporate environments (Lisa) and small publishing (Charlotte's company), not just learning contexts. The Handbook foregrounds learning-context examples; the podcast widens to organisational examples. The pattern libraries' "of general application" claim (per IDENTIFY) is supported by this widening.

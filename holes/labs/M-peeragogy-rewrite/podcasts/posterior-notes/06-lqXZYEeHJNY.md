## Section 6 — Operational content

- **Pattern-language ancestor named explicitly.**  Christopher Alexander's *15 properties of wholeness* are shown on screen and discussed as a frame for life patterns.  This is the genealogical ancestor of the entire peeragogy pattern library claim (the Handbook's chapter 21 invokes Alexander; ep 6 actually reads from him).  Worth carrying forward: **the rubric's pattern-language claim (2.6) becomes more concrete when the genealogy is visible in voice, not just cited in text.**
- **Visual thinking as a deliberate discipline.**  Amanda: *"there was one point in my career trajectory at which I said to myself, I am determined to learn how to think visually."*  Naming the cultivation of a mode of thinking as itself a peeragogical move.  Adjacent to **6.0a (curating-not-experting)** but distinct: where curating-not-experting is about *stance*, this is about *cultivated capacity*.
- **6.0a (curating-not-experting) exhibited.**  Joe lightly facilitates between Amanda and Nura without claiming expertise in either's domain; brings each into conversation; integrates by occasionally reflecting back what he heard.  Strong working example.
- **Boundaries as a primary visualization framing.**  Nura: *"They're always operating within boundaries... what is the inside and what is the outside?"*  Pairs with Paola's body-as-first-territory framing in ep 5 — second independent voicing of a *territorial* register for peeragogy practice.

## Section 7 — What would update this prior?

- **Recurrence in 2026 contexts.**  Climate policy (Nura), visual-practice consulting (Amanda), Emacs research groups (where Joe and Nura met).  Three new contexts the rubric could test against.

- **Critical frame.**  Joe surfaces a recurring criticism of the Handbook itself: *"one of the criticisms of the handbook I think has often been not that it's not visual looking, but that it's just sometimes confusing and is all this stuff in there very well expressed."*  Direct in-voice evidence that the Handbook's writing-clarity is a known concern; bears on carrier-shape — a chapter-by-chapter rewrite (option (a)) would inherit this concern; a zine-per-pattern carrier (option (b)) might address it by enforcing one-clear-point-per-zine.
- **Form-claims tested.**  The PAR-moved-to-after-party form is now stable across four consecutive episodes (3, 4, 5, 6).  The form-change is settled, not experimental.
- **Practice-supporting-doctrine analogy.**  Amanda's *"art is healing... my daily walk in the park completely shifts me"* names embodied practice as central — pairs with Paola's body-as-first-territory (ep 5).  Two episodes in a row foreground embodiment.  The practice-supporting-doctrine analogy continues to gain texture: not just gatherings and rituals (sangha), but explicitly *embodied* gatherings and rituals.

## Additions to the prior (ep 6)

- **A1-ep6 (Christopher Alexander's 15 properties of wholeness named in voice).**  Pattern-language genealogy made visible.  The Handbook cites Alexander; ep 6 actually reads from his work.  The rubric's pattern-language claim (2.6) gets concrete corroboration.
- **A2-ep6 (visual thinking as deliberate discipline).**  Amanda's "I am determined to learn how to think visually" names the cultivation of a mode as a peeragogical move.  Adjacent to but distinct from curating-not-experting (6.0a).
- **A3-ep6 (patterns are not categorically good).**  Joe: *"we shouldn't necessarily look at patterns only as things that we, as we like, we can get patterns of abuse or patterns of negative thinking, all kinds of things come in patterns."*  The Handbook's chapter 21 (patterns) and chapter 22 (antipatterns) implicitly classify: peeragogy patterns are good, antipatterns are bad.  Ep 6 names a more critical relationship: pattern-recognition itself is morally neutral; the practice's job is to be critical of which patterns it adopts and which it avoids.  **Promoted to rubric (see below).**
- **A4-ep6 (aesthetics of writing as a peeragogy concern).**  Joe: *"a kind of aesthetics of writing into the work itself as a whole work is one of the ways in which we could use visual thinking above and beyond petitioning you for more drawings."*  Echoes A4-ep3 (pattern-cross-reference enacted in voice, not just claimed) at a different scale: the form of the Handbook's writing is itself a peeragogy concern, not separate from content.
- **A5-ep6 (boundaries as primary visualization framing).**  Nura's articulation pairs with Paola's body-as-first-territory (ep 5).  Two independent voicings of a *territorial* register for peeragogy practice.  Worth carrying as evidence the territorial framing is a recurring concern.
- **A6-ep6 (adding perspective = gaining awareness).**  Amanda: *"the second we add another perspective, we've gained awareness of some kind. And the more awareness we gain, the more we can show up in the world in a grounded place as ourselves, fully."*  Names the meta-mechanism by which polycentric practice (rubric claim 3.1) actually delivers: it's not about the perspectives being equal but about *additional* perspectives compounding awareness.
- **A7-ep6 (the Handbook is criticized for being confusing — direct in-voice evidence).**  Useful for the carrier-shape decision: a chapter-by-chapter rewrite (option (a)) inherits this concern; a zine-per-pattern carrier (option (b)) might enforce one-clear-point-per-zine as a structural answer.
- **A8-ep6 (Joe re-states the v4 intention again).**  Same wish as ep 4.  The recurring statement-of-intent without subsequent action is itself the stewardship-succession gap (rubric item 3.9) surfaced in real time across episodes.

## Promoted to rubric

**A3-ep6 (patterns are not categorically good)** is the highest-leverage single addition; it fills a gap in the rubric's section 4 (boundary by negation), which currently has no post-2016 additions, and it is general-purpose — applicable to any pattern-language practice's relationship to its own vocabulary.

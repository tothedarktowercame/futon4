The first episode focused specifically on **how to facilitate
disagreement** in peer learning — a topic the 2016 Handbook touches on
in chapter 14 (Co-Facilitation, the Heron quote about hierarchical /
cooperative / autonomous modes) but doesn't operationalise.  Richard's
DEI-consulting practice supplies named operational moves: *calling-in
not calling-out*, *brave-safe space*, *WAIT (Why Am I Talking)*,
*cognitive entrenchment under pressure*, an explicit five-element
formula for facilitating challenging conversations.  Joe surfaces a
useful reframe at the end: *difficult* vs *challenging* — the latter
is rewarding, the former is just hard.  Charlotte closes with a
vulnerable disclosure that the topic was personally chosen for a
family situation she's been avoiding; the practice serves personal
need, not abstract curiosity.

PAR returns to after-party (continuing eps 1–7 form, after the eps 8+9
PAR-free pattern in the outward-facing accelerator/course episodes).

## Section 6 — Operational content

- **6.0a (curating-not-experting) exhibited.**  Joe facilitates very lightly between two clear practitioners; brings each in sequentially; doesn't position himself as DEI-expert.
- **PAR returns to after-party.**  Eps 8+9 omitted PAR; ep 10 brings it back as the after-party content.  The pattern emerging: PAR is bound to *core-crew/conversation-guest* episodes, not to *outward-facing/format-experiment* episodes (eps 8+9 were the accelerator + course episodes).
- **Newcomer pattern.**  Joe explicitly names how the participants are at different newcomer-distances from each other (Joe-Charlotte long, Joe-Judith newer, Joe-Richard newest).  Conscious surfacing of newcomer-state in voice.
- **NEW operational move: Calling-in (not calling-out).**  Richard's named discipline.  Distinct from inviting (which is gentler) and from confrontation (which is harder).  *"I never call them out, always call them in."*  Operational stance for disagreement-friendly facilitation.  **Promoted to rubric (see below).**
- **NEW concrete tool: WAIT (Why Am I Talking).**  Richard's facilitator self-discipline acronym.  Adjacent to but distinct from listening-as-discipline (added ep 7 in posterior notes).
- **NEW operational structure: Awareness + Storytelling + Education + Historical-Facts + Action.**  Richard's five-element formula for facilitating challenging conversations.  Could become a flexiarg.

## Section 7 — What would update this prior?

- **Recurrence in 2026 contexts.**  DEI consulting (Richard, 18 years), American Chemical Society leadership (Judith), US Rowing DEI work (Richard).  Multiple 2020s peer-facilitation contexts where calling-in / brave-safe-space discipline is operational.
- **Adjacent vocabularies.**  *Calling-in / calling-out* (DEI literature, social-justice activism).  *Brave-safe space* (DEI / pedagogy literature).  *Cognitive entrenchment* (decision-making research).  *Confirmation bias / group-think* (cognitive psychology, named operationally).  *Seek first to understand* (Franklin Covey).  *Brené Brown's clarity-is-kindness*.  All adjacent to peeragogy's operational vocabulary; none of them are in the Handbook.
- **Critical frame.**  Charlotte's vulnerable disclosure at episode close: *"I have a difficult situation, a family situation that you know, I've been avoiding. So this partly was bringing this topic in as a podcast topic was I was hoping to get some practical, you know, I'm just afraid to bring it up."*  The practice serves personal need; this is the strongest in-voice evidence so far that *the practitioners themselves use the practice for their own lives*, not just for the project's purposes.
- **Form-claims tested.**  Joe's *difficult vs challenging* distinction at episode close — *"a conversation that's difficult for no good reason and a conversation that's challenging, but ultimately really rewarding. That reframes the whole thing for me."*  Reframe of the topic itself; bears on how the practice's vocabulary is presented.
- **Practice-supporting-doctrine analogy.**  DEI facilitation is a practice tradition (consciousness-raising lineage, Civil Rights Movement formation work, queer affinity groups, etc.) that has its own non-text-canonical sustaining infrastructure.  Same family as sangha, Quaker meeting, AA — practices that survive without canonical texts because they're embodied in facilitator skill and gathering format.

## Additions to the prior (ep 10)

- **A1-ep10 (calling-in not calling-out as operational facilitator stance).**  Richard's explicit naming of the move.  Distinct from existing patterns; specifically designed for disagreement-friendly facilitation.  **Promoted to rubric (see below).**
- **A2-ep10 (brave-safe space).**  Distinct from "safe space" alone — names that the safety is for *being brave*, not for *being unchallenged*.  Operational vocabulary.
- **A3-ep10 (vulnerability as structural blocker for challenging conversations).**  Richard: *"the reason why people have a difficult time with challenging conversations is because they're afraid of being vulnerable. And they're afraid of stepping in it."*  Names *why* peer learning around hard topics is hard — not skill gap, but vulnerability-aversion.
- **A4-ep10 (cognitive entrenchment under pressure).**  Richard cites a decision-making finding: people become cognitively entrenched when pressured.  Bears on facilitator design — pressure produces the opposite of what facilitators want.
- **A5-ep10 (WAIT — Why Am I Talking).**  Concrete facilitator-self-discipline acronym.  Adjacent to listening-as-discipline (ep 7 posterior notes addition).
- **A6-ep10 (Awareness + Storytelling + Education + Historical-Facts + Action formula).**  Richard's five-element operational structure for facilitating challenging conversations.  Could become a flexiarg in `futon3/library/peeragogy/`.
- **A7-ep10 (online may be easier for some difficult conversations — counter-intuitive).**  Richard finds online *easier* for facilitating DEI work because body-language-as-distraction is reduced.  Significant for the 2016 *online co-learning* framing (rubric 2.5) — extends rather than qualifies it.
- **A8-ep10 (difficult vs challenging distinction).**  Joe's reframe at episode close.  *Difficult* = hard for no good reason; *challenging* = hard but ultimately rewarding.  Reframes how the practice's stakes are presented.
- **A9-ep10 (Charlotte's vulnerable-disclosure as meta-evidence the practice serves personal need).**  Charlotte chose this topic because of a family situation she's been avoiding.  The practitioners *use the practice for themselves*, not just for the project.  Strongest in-voice evidence so far for this register.

## Promoted to rubric

**A1-ep10 (calling-in not calling-out as operational facilitator stance)** is the highest-leverage single addition; it names a specific operational move the Handbook doesn't have, distinct from everything in the existing pattern library, with concrete in-voice evidence (Richard's 550-person workshop story).

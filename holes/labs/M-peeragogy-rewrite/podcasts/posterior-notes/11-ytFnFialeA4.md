A guest interview about EmacsConf — an annual two-day virtual
conference run by ~10 volunteer organizers (kernel of ~4) on a weekly
Saturday meeting cadence, with ~300–400 concurrent viewers across two
days.  Substantive content on **organizing methodology** rather than
peeragogy in the abstract: check-in discipline at meeting start,
real-time collaborative pad as participatory feedback infrastructure,
the *locomotive metaphor* (pulse-energy contributors need a kernel of
regulars to sustain the show year after year), and an
anti-expertise-inflation discipline for mixed-level workshops (*"toe
down the level when they're being too technical"*).  Joe gives a
direct in-voice account of how he came to peeragogy via the Emacs
mailing list — formative origin material for the M-peeragogy-rewrite
mission.

## Additions to the prior (ep 11)

- **A1-ep11 (check-in discipline at start of meetings).**  Operational move from EmacsConf organizing.  Distinct from PAR (which closes) — this opens.  *"making sure that people say where they are at mentally"* before doing the agenda.  The Handbook's chapter 14 (Co-Facilitation) discusses moderation generally; this is a more specific named operational move.  **Promoted to rubric (see below).**
- **A2-ep11 (real-time collaborative pad as participatory feedback infrastructure).**  Concrete carrier-(e) instance: ~200 concurrent contributors writing into a shared pad during the event, then 3-4 weeks of post-event extraction shaping the next year's process.  Bears on the carrier-shape decision in DERIVE — practice-supporting infrastructure can be very low-tech if there's a shared pad with a maintained editing practice.
- **A3-ep11 ("toe down the level" anti-expertise-inflation discipline).**  Operational move for mixed-level workshops where maintainers' technical specificity could lose beginners.  Adjacent to but distinct from curating-not-experting (6.0a) — that's about facilitator stance; this is about peer-to-peer moderation of register inside the gathering.
- **A4-ep11 (locomotive metaphor: pulse-energy contributors need a kernel of regulars).**  Refines rubric 3.9 (stewardship-succession): succession isn't just naming a successor; it's maintaining a small kernel (~3-5 regulars) on a recurring cadence (weekly Saturdays for EmacsConf) that carries the practice across pulse-energy contributors who join for specific events.  Names the *minimum sustainable kernel* as the structural variable.
- **A5-ep11 (quantitative growth ≠ diversity growth).**  Leo's honest observation extends rubric 3.12 (diversity-not-expertise) into a constructive claim: doubling attendance doesn't deliver doubling diversity; the practice must work on diversity *explicitly* and continuously, not as a side-effect of growth.
- **A6-ep11 (free software as a sustaining paradigm, not just a license).**  Leo articulates free software's value as a *paradigm* — openness, transparency, version-controlled change history.  Extends rubric 3.2 (open licensing) into a more substantive paradigm-level claim.
- **A7-ep11 (Joe's personal Purogogy origin story via Emacs mailing list).**  *"I would post things on this mailing list, and the people were so generous, they would send back comments saying, that was pretty good, but here you can do it better. And so like, actually not only was that how I learned how to program, that was for me really one of the key foundations of Purogogy."*  Direct historical evidence about how Joe came to peeragogy work — formative-origin-material for M-peeragogy-rewrite IDENTIFY/ARGUE.  Worth carrying forward to ARGUE: peeragogy is partly *what Joe lived through and named*.
- **A8-ep11 (Emacs Workshops as recurring local meetings — Linux User Groups lineage).**  Concrete carrier-(e) instance with explicit ancestor — recurring local cohort gathering format that has worked across decades in a different domain (Linux/free-software).

## Section 6 — Operational content

- **Wrapper pattern *explicitly named in voice* and applied to a new medium.** Charles: *"One of the ways that we wrap kind of using a peeragogy pattern, our form of wrap is a video remix or edit distillation."* The Handbook describes Wrapper; the podcast names it by its pattern name and shows a concrete instance. **This is the rubric's pattern-language claim (2.6 + 5.4) being enacted out loud, not just claimed.** (See A4-ep3.)
- **Heartbeat pattern.** *"Central Europe time five o'clock in the afternoon on Sundays"* — explicit recurring rhythm sustaining the Cool Laboratory.
- **Newcomer pattern.** The Trello parent-dashboard is graduated-entry tooling for newcomer parents — *"a quick start guide … so it's almost like a quick start guide for parents on activities."*
- **Roles pattern.** Curators (Charles + Lauren), host (Charlie), tech director (Joe), producer (Charlotte) — all named operationally.
- **PAR** — moves out of the broadcast into the Jitsi after-party. Form-change deliberate (*"This time we're gonna change it up in reaction. We're not gonna do it during the podcast itself"*).
- **NEW pattern candidate: Curating-not-experting.** Charles: *"we're not experts, we're curators and we're bringing a lot of people into conversation and sort of connective intelligence."* The position relative to expertise is itself a peeragogical move. Distinct from the Roles pattern (which is about *which* roles); this is about *what stance the role takes toward expertise*. Candidate for a new flexiarg, candidate for the rubric (see promotion below).

## Section 7 — What would update this prior?

- **Recurrence in 2026 contexts.** Kiko Lab + Cool Laboratory + Learning Garden are concrete 2020s peeragogy-named instances. *Pandemic-substitute education* as a context where peeragogy's operational moves are visibly necessary. **Sister Circle Metacogs** also named — adjacent practice the same circle runs.
- **Adjacent vocabularies.** *Collective intelligence* / *connective intelligence* (Charles uses both). *Crap detection* (Hemingway via Rheingold). *Metacognition* (Scott Moreing's distinctions module). *Unschooling / unlearning* (Charles). All adjacent to peeragogy's named moves.
- **Form-claims tested.** PAR-moved-off-broadcast (A6-ep3) is a real signal: the public-facing artifact and the practice's reflexive layer have different audiences. The 2016 form-claim (5.4 self-application performed) assumed they'd be the same surface.
- **Critical frame.** Lauren's surprise that parents are the hardest to recruit is honest engagement with where the practice's reach falls short. *"I thought every parent in the world wanted a cool international group of kids who would meet and get to know each other and learn about each other's culture and learn to collaborate together. That was my dream, but it's been difficult."*
- **Practice-supporting-doctrine analogy.** Strong: the Cool Laboratory IS practice-supporting infrastructure (recurring Sunday session, parent dashboard, video wraps). The Handbook isn't in the room; the practice is.

## Additions to the prior (ep 3)

- **A1-ep3 (full spectrum: peer learning + peer MAKING).** Charles: *"the peer making and producing part maybe gets left out more in the conversation about Peeragogy."* Refines rubric claim 2.2. Distinct from Roles (about *which* roles people take) — this is about *what disposition the role takes toward expertise*. **Promoted to rubric (see below).**

The Handbook claims pattern-language status (rubric 2.6); the podcast performs it audibly. Worth noting that the Handbook's silent claim becomes a podcast's spoken move.

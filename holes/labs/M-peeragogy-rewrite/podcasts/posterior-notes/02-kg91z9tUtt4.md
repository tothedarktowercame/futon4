## Section 6 — Operational content

- **Newcomer pattern** — Pete's *"I really like to encourage people to do … expand your understanding of how to read Wikipedia a little bit more critically. So the two things that you can really learn a lot from … is the View History tab on any article and also the Talk tab on any article"* is a textbook Newcomer-pattern instance: graduated entry with low-cost first moves.
- **Roles pattern** — Pete is the campaign-builder, Lane is the embedded-institution liaison, Joe is the framer; named operationally throughout.
- **Discerning-a-Pattern** — Pete's articulation of the *empowerment shift* (see Additions, A2-ep2) IS a pattern-discernment move applied to Wikipedia training.
- **PAR (Paragogical Action Review)** — applied again. The five steps walk cleanly: review intention → establish what's happening → perspectives → what we learned/changed → what to change going forward.
- **collaboration-coherence/misunderstanding-power-laws** (positive form) — the Daily Mail deprecation illustrates the Wiki community handling source-quality via deliberation-with-evidence rather than power-law arbitration.

## Section 7 — What would update this prior?

- **Recurrence in 2026 contexts.** Wikipedia is the dominant case named — and it predates and outlasts the 2016 Handbook. **News on Wiki**, **WikiCite**, **Wikidata**, **Federated Wiki** (Mike Caulfield's project) all named as live 2026 infrastructure. Outward MAP should follow at least News on Wiki and Federated Wiki.
- **Adjacent vocabularies.** Wikipedia editorial vocabulary (talk page, edit history, deprecation, civility policy, Wikimedian-in-Residence) overlaps substantially with peeragogy patterns. The podcast doesn't translate; the audience is assumed to know neither vocabulary. ARGUE could surface this overlap deliberately.
- **Form-claims tested.** The "decentralised on social axis, centralised on ontological axis" insight is a real refinement of the Handbook's polycentric claim; should land in DERIVE if a carrier-shape decision involves the Handbook becoming a co-produced wiki-style artifact.
- **Critical frame.** Strong evidence: Pete on educated-white-male editor demographic; Lane on language disparities + civility-not-yet-solved. The 2016 rubric's critical frame stays live in 2026; both critiques are *raised by Wikipedians themselves*, not external opponents. Worth carrying that texture forward.
- **Practice-supporting-doctrine analogy.** Wikipedia is a striking case for the analogy: 25-year-old practice, no central authoritative text, sustained by community editorial discipline that's adapted from external sources (academia). The practice has clearly outlived any single canonical-text moment. *This bears on whether peeragogy needs a Handbook at all, or whether it needs Wikipedia-shaped infrastructure for its own practice.*

## Additions to the prior (claims surfaced by ep 2)

- **A1-ep2 (peeragogy by any other name).** Joe: *"I would call it pure Gaji inaction, but I would say pure Gaji by any other name."* Wikipedia explicitly named as enacting peeragogy. This is more than the Foreword's "peer production has built Wikipedia and GNU/Linux" gesture — it claims peeragogy-the-vocabulary names a thing already-named-otherwise. Significant for the carrier-shape decision: if peeragogy already has a 25-year successful instance under another name, the Handbook's job may be less to build the practice and more to articulate what the existing instance has implicitly been doing.
- **A2-ep2 (empowerment from criticism).** Pete: *"their disposition towards [Wikipedia bias] shifts a bit from merely outrage to outrage with a sense that there might be something I can do about it. … It's inherently empowering because once you realize you can do something about it, that's actually true."* The move *outrage → agency* is a specific peeragogical mechanism the Handbook doesn't name. Connects to Newcomer pattern (graduated entry) AND to the collaboration-coherence/messy-with-lurkers positive form (acknowledging-the-problem-with-an-action-surface).
- **A3-ep2 (ethics is the bottleneck).** Lane: *"What I'm not sure is gonna get sorted out is the right ethical foundation for all of this. That's where we really need help."* The 2016 rubric (claim 1.3) frames methods as the next bottleneck after tech. Lane in 2026 frames *ethics* as the next bottleneck after methods. Significant trajectory: tech → methods → ethics. ARGUE should hold this.
- **A4-ep2 (Federated Wiki as adjacent model).** Pete cites Mike Caulfield's Federated Wiki as an alternative-to-Wikipedia model: each contributor has their own site, easy fork-and-merge. *"I don't think you could really create something like Wikipedia through that model, because you're getting away from that centralization that is kind of the strength."* A real adjacent vocabulary in the wiki/co-learning space; worth a MAP-outward thread.
- **A5-ep2 (decentralised-on-one-axis-centralised-on-another).** Pete's articulation of Wikipedia's structural duality refines the Handbook's flat "polycentric" framing. Practical implication: any carrier the rewrite chooses needs to settle which axes are centralised and which decentralised. A zine-per-pattern carrier (option (b)) is centralised in topic-naming but decentralised in distribution; a practice-supporting-infrastructure carrier (option (e)) is decentralised in instance but centralised in shared rituals.

# M-or-training-as-learning-system

Evaluating open-research training as a learning system: closing the gap between what the ORP evaluation could establish on its own evidence and what UKRN-S's design commitments need to be evidence-grounded.

## 1. IDENTIFY

### The gap

The ORP evaluation established, with reasonable confidence, that *institutional architecture* — local support structures, role coordination, evaluation feedback loops — determines whether open-research training takes root in any individual institution. This is the central qualitative finding, consistent with NPT-grounded coding of the four T3 focus-group transcripts.

The ORP evaluation did *not* establish four things that UKRN-S's design decisions now depend on. We name these as `sorry`s — proof obligations the predecessor programme could not discharge.

**Sorry 1. Researcher-level behaviour change.** The T1 evaluation wave was planned but has not been run. Whether participants in trained institutions actually adopt open-research practices remains evidentially open.

**Sorry 2. Cross-institutional evidence flow during the work, not just at the end.** The ORP did have evaluation capability — focus-group analysis, role-codebook construction, the population model — but the evaluation happened largely at the *end* of the programme rather than continuously throughout it. The role-codebook analysis showed that the *evaluation-analysis* role (`EV_analyse`) was absent across all four focus-group transcripts at the moments those transcripts captured. The ORP did not have, at any in-flight point, an operational capability for comparing what worked at one institution against what worked at another in a way that could feed back into ongoing decisions. Evidence stayed local; integration was retrospective.

**Sorry 3. Tactical institutional participation in evaluation.** Institutions contributed to programme-level evaluation but did not drive evaluation of their own local contexts. Participation was strategic ("we ran the training; here are attendance figures") rather than tactical ("here is what changed in our institution as a result, and here is what would have to change for it to stick").

**Sorry 4. Evidence-grounded basis for the design commitments UKRN-S faces.** The working paper synthesising the ORP evidence carries candidate design commitments — a learning-system architecture; six tensions held in the open under evidence; specific bootstrap-phase actions. These are *recommendations* in the WP. They are not yet *verified* recommendations. The WP carries them as candidate framings; the question of which earn their place under evidence is open.

UKRN-S is a Community Interest Company that has just inherited the wind-down of the ORP. It faces design decisions on a calendar (Elle's planning meeting is in May 2026) before any of the four sorries are closed by additional empirical work. The mission's gap is the gap between *what ORP evaluation could establish on its own evidence* and *what UKRN-S's commitments need to be evidence-grounded*.

The mission's job is to close that gap as far as it can be closed without waiting for fresh data — by treating the WP's candidate framings as verification candidates, by producing verdicts on each, and by specifying the bootstrap-phase actions that produce the additional evidence needed to close the remaining sorries in follow-on missions.

### What closing the gap looks like

The gap closes when each of the four sorries has a status:

- *Closed*, with an evidence trail recorded in the mission, OR
- *In closure*, with a bootstrap-phase action specified that produces the missing evidence, OR
- *Deferred*, with a follow-on mission scoped to address it.

The gap closes when each candidate framing carried in the WP has a verification verdict (*robust*; *robust under stated conditions*; *revised*; *set aside*) with the empirical evidence named.

The gap closes when UKRN-S's recommended design commitments are stated with each commitment grounded in named evidence — not in a candidate framing, and not in the WP's rhetoric.

The gap closes when a plain-language argument exists, accessible to a UKRN-S board member, a funder, or a member-institution lead without prior engagement with the framework.

### Theoretical anchoring

The mission draws on framings each treated as falsifiable, not as foundational commitments.

- **Normalisation Process Theory** (May & Finch 2009). The grammar the ORP transcripts were coded against. The codebook is anchored for this mission; what its outputs imply about the institutional landscape is what the verification work has to settle.
- **Pattern theory in the architectural-design tradition** (Alexander 1977; with the WP's adaptation to institutional design). Five candidate institution-level patterns and three programme-level patterns, treated as binding-constraint diagnostics under verification.
- **Active inference** (Friston). The cyber-ant simulation in the WP recasts the population model as an active-inference agent. Carried as a *verification tool*, not a foundational commitment to active inference as the right account of UKRN-S behaviour.
- **The Krowne sustainability triangle** (Krowne 2003) and its tetrahedral extension carried in the WP. Candidate framing for how local sustainability becomes network-scale through a shareable-evidence apex.
- **The MVSG.** The WP's geometric synthesis of UKRN-S as evidence apex above three institutional implementation modes. Candidate under verification.

A *set aside* verdict on any of these is allowed and would be a finding in its own right.

## 2. MAP

### Frame: relative to the UKRN Working Paper stack

This MAP works relative to the existing UKRN Working Paper series, of which this mission's eventual contribution is a candidate next entry. The relevant prior is **UKRN Working Paper 01** (Grange et al., 2023, DOI 10.31219/osf.io/s2f6k): a survey of training resources and priorities across 11 UKRN institutions, producing a ranked wish list of 55+ items in six categories, 591 catalogued resources, and three high-level recommendations. Our work adds qualitative evaluation evidence (ORP T3 focus-group analysis, role codebook, population model, candidate framings carried in the npt working paper) on top of that prior survey work.

We have, in Joe's phrasing, *been through this exercise once* — the survey-and-publish-as-WP pattern is established within UKRN. This mission is the next iteration in that pattern, with two extensions: (a) qualitative evaluation evidence WP1 did not have, and (b) T1 delivery counts (2024–2026) that WP1 anticipated as part of the Programme's third evidence layer but did not yet have.

### Input → Process → Output: three triangulating tracks

The UKRN Working Paper stack — and the wider sector context UKRN-S operates in — splits into three tracks for MAP purposes. The *training* track answers "what training to deliver, and does it land?"; the *indicators* track answers "can we measure OR practice change at population level, and what does change actually look like?"; the *sector-priorities* track answers "where does OR / open-research training sit in the wider UK research-culture and university-sector frame?". Each track has at least one anchor datapoint pair we can model as input → process → output.

In futon5 typological terms, the three tracks map onto **GENOTYPE** (training — the substrate-level work UKRN-S directly trains on), **EXOTYPE** (indicators — the externally-observable expression contractors will demand demonstrable change in), and **XENOTYPE** (sector priorities — the wider environment UKRN-S has to be legible within). Coherence across all three is required for the design to land.

#### Training track — pair 1

##### UKRN WP1 / Open Research Training Resources and Priorities (2023)

- *Input.* Institutional Lead survey across 11 UKRN partner institutions; ~3 months of iterative consultation (2022); informal discussions and reviews of existing work to establish a framework before the survey ran.
- *Process.* Systematic categorisation of training resources and priorities into a wish list, structured along the research lifecycle (Planning → Conducting → Analysing → Disseminating → Evaluating) plus Incentive Structures. Each item carried (a) the training resources available and used at the institution, and (b) the priority the institution accorded that item.
- *Output.* 55+-item wish list (numbered 1–55+ across six categories), 591 catalogued resources, three high-value recommendations: develop open-access training materials (only 10.5% of resources were open-access at the time); engage professional services colleagues and coordinate across institutions; prioritise gaps in academic incentive structures (recruitment, assessment/evaluation, promotion).

##### T1 delivery counts (2024–2026)

- *Input.* Quarterly self-reports from institutional trainers (T3s); 24 institutions; six quarters of recorded data plus partial seventh-quarter data, with the data window starting "Start to October 2024" and running through "April 2026 to June 2026".
- *Process.* Per-institution, per-quarter count aggregation; overall total against an internal target; running per-institution totals; institutions self-report attendance counts (number of trainees attending T1 instances), distinct from number of T1 instances or number of T3 courses informing the instances.
- *Output.* 2,816 T1 trainees trained to date; 104% of target; quarterly average 397.6 trainees; uneven institutional distribution: Newcastle 482, King's College London 262, Bristol 249, Cardiff 247, Sheffield 222, Oxford Brookes 206, Southampton 157, Keele 143, Surrey 120, Manchester 108, Wolverhampton 101 — and a long tail of institutions with two-digit or single-digit counts (Aberdeen 0, East Anglia 3, Royal Veterinary College 3, Glasgow 15, Leicester 36, Oxford 40).

##### Together — a delivery-viability layer

The training-track pair is a *delivery-viability* signal in two parts: WP1 specified what to deliver and what resources/priorities existed; the T1 counts confirm delivery happened, at network scale. The 104%-of-target signal says the training reached the people the Programme intended; the per-institution variance shows that delivery is real but uneven.

Neither datapoint, on its own evidence, addresses *architectural sustainability*. WP1 surveyed priorities in 2022 — before substantial training was delivered. The T1 counts measure delivery (trainees attended) but not outcomes (trainees adopted practices). The architectural-sustainability layer requires the qualitative evidence the ORP T3 focus groups produced, plus the planned T1 wave (qualitative interviews; distinct from the T1 counts) and the indicators-track work below.

#### Indicators track — pair 2

##### UKRN WP02 / Open Research Indicators: sector priorities (June 2023)

- *Input.* Survey of UKRN institutions and individuals in the UK academic research sector to establish which aspects of OR should be priorities for monitoring; 14 institutions to participate in pilot projects; 29 responses received.
- *Process.* Categorisation of 83 discrete priorities identified in the survey responses; ranking; selection of priority aspects suitable for indicator development.
- *Output.* 12 priority aspects of OR for monitoring: sharing research data; sharing research software; FAIR research; Open Access to publications; OR beyond publications; persistent identifiers; metadata and technical standards; links across the scholarly record; pre-registration; reward and recognition; training and awareness; equality, diversity and inclusion. WP02's role is *agenda-setting*: it tells the indicator pilots which aspects of OR to attempt to measure.

##### UKRN WP09 / Open Research Indicators Final Report (February 2026)

- *Input.* Six pilot projects, voluntarily conducted by academic and professional staff alongside international data-service providers, across six aspects of OR: FAIR data; open data; data availability statements (DAS); downstream effects of sharing; pre-registration; CRediT taxonomy. Pilots used institutional and third-party datasets; assessed automated extraction methods; compared provider outputs with human validation.
- *Process.* Each pilot operationalised a candidate measurement approach; produced findings on tool maturity, data availability, and inter-method agreement; compared automated against manual validation. Pilot leads convened periodically; appendices document each pilot in detail.
- *Output.* A mixed verdict on automated indicator feasibility:
  - **Operational at pilot scale**: pre-registration detection (registry traces); selected components of FAIRness (persistent-identifier detection, metadata completeness); CRediT statement detection (with further refinement).
  - **Achievable but limited**: most indicators require continued tool development; current sector workflows and metadata standards are not yet mature enough for reliable, automated institutional monitoring.
  - **Manual checking still necessary**: both for accuracy and for validation of emerging automated approaches.
  - **Cross-cutting recommendation**: coordinated development of shared definitions, taxonomies, and data standards is needed, ideally led by a trusted international body (the report points at the Open Science Monitoring Initiative — OSMI).
  - **Operational caveat**: the work was unfunded; the report flags that future work of this scale should be fully resourced and centrally coordinated.

##### Together — an architectural-sustainability layer (in part)

The indicators-track pair is what the *Visible Impact* candidate pattern (in the npt WP) requires for operational substrate. Visible Impact's gate factors in the population model are systematisation (NPT 4.1) and communal-appraisal (NPT 4.2); both are about how OR practice change becomes legible at the institution and network scales. WP02 sets the agenda for that legibility (12 priority aspects); WP09 reports on whether the agenda is currently buildable (partially: some aspects are operational at pilot scale; most aren't yet).

This pair is *directly relevant to the UKRN-S commercial proposition*. Joe's framing: contracting institutions look for *demonstrable change*, not endorsement. The indicators-track work is the substrate that makes "demonstrable" possible. WP09 is honest about what's currently demonstrable: pre-registration, FAIRness components, and CRediT — at pilot scale, with manual validation. Anything beyond that is "achievable but not yet operational." A UKRN-S contract that promises demonstrable change has to be calibrated against this current state.

#### Sector-priorities track — pair 3 (XENOTYPE)

##### Enhancing Research Culture: UK sector priorities and challenges (Sept 2022; Jacobs and Wilson, UKRN + University of Bristol)

- *Input.* UKRN- and Bristol-hosted events with UK universities in summer 2022; participants encouraged to speak freely; broad cross-sector attendance from academic and professional staff working on research-culture initiatives. Catalysed in part by Research England's £28m research-culture funding allocation (2021–22).
- *Process.* Synthesis of discussion themes; categorisation under broad headings; identification of common challenges and patterns of work across UK institutions; explicit framing of research culture as "the behaviours, values, expectations, attitudes and norms of our research communities" (UKRN/RoyalSoc working definition).
- *Output.* Eight broad themes for sector-level research-culture work: equality, diversity and inclusion (EDI); individual support; system change; staff development; leadership; research practice (where OR / open and transparent research sits); engagement beyond higher education; and a cross-cutting note that institutions are only one actor in a wider system that includes funders, REF, and regulation. Recurring observation: "ringfenced funding has been instrumental for institutions to work on internal or small collaborative activities" — i.e., the sector treats research-culture work as funder-catalysed rather than spontaneously prioritised.

##### Opportunity, growth and partnership: A blueprint for change from the UK's universities (Sept 2024; Universities UK)

- *Input.* UUK member-institution consultation across the UK university sector; sector-positioning work; foreword by UUK President Dame Sally Mapstone framing the moment as "a fork in the road in the history of the UK's universities."
- *Process.* Eight-chapter structuring of UK-university-sector concerns into a strategic blueprint addressed to government and funders; sector-level priority articulation; recommendations for policy and resourcing.
- *Output.* Eight strategic chapters: expanding opportunity; more responsive and collaborative tertiary education; generating local growth; a world-leading research and innovation system; global reach, reputation and impact; putting universities on firm financial footing; better regulation; improving how the impact of universities is assessed. Chapter 4 (research and innovation) and chapter 8 (impact assessment) are the most directly relevant to OR / indicators work. Sets the policy frame: universities as agents of change, lifelong learning, impact-assessment reform, financial sustainability.

##### Together — a sector-positioning layer (XENOTYPE)

The XENOTYPE pair tells UKRN-S where it sits in the wider UK research-culture and university-sector frame. Two findings.

First, *OR is one of seven-or-eight competing sector priorities for research culture*. The 2022 Enhancing Research Culture work names OR (under "research practice") as one priority among many — alongside EDI, leadership, system change, staff development, engagement-beyond-HE. UKRN-S's commercial proposition has to be legible against this distribution; it cannot assume OR is the only thing institutions care about. Where contracting institutions weigh research-culture spend, OR-training competes for attention with EDI initiatives, leadership programmes, and assessment reform.

Second, *impact-assessment reform is on the policy frame*. The 2024 UUK blueprint includes an entire chapter on improving how the impact of universities is assessed. UKRN-S's indicators-track work (the EXOTYPE pair: WP02 + WP09) sits inside this wider impact-assessment-reform conversation. The OSMI mention in WP09 is one node in that wider conversation. Where UKRN-S can plug into the impact-assessment-reform frame, the indicators work has more sector-level traction.

The mission's design (DERIVE) has to be coherent across all three tracks: training that produces measurable change that is legible to the wider sector. Each axis has named failure modes in ORP/UKRN-related work: training without indicators (delivery without verification, the *Visible Impact* gap of the npt WP); indicators without sector-frame integration (measurement without policy traction); sector-frame without training (positioning without product). The mission's design has to address all three.

#### Methodology note: scattering experiment

What this MAP does methodologically is a *scattering experiment*. Each I-O pair from the UKRN and sector documents — input observable, output observable — gets lifted to an I-P-O theory by hypothesising the process P that produced O from I. The hypothesised processes are not assumed; they are candidate theories about how the source organisation worked, derivable from the recorded inputs and outputs and checkable against further data.

The scattering frame:
- We fire probes (I-O pairs) at the latent organisational substrate.
- Each probe scatters — produces an output we can measure against the recorded input.
- The deflection (the gap between input and output) is the work of P.
- Triangulating across probes (training, indicators, sector) reconstructs a multi-angle view of the substrate.

This is why three probes is better than one. A single I-P-O theory could be a confabulation; three pairs that scatter consistently against different angles let us check the lift's coherence. The futon5 typology is the angle classification: GENOTYPE (training), EXOTYPE (indicators), XENOTYPE (sector priorities) — three different incidence angles on the same substrate.

The methodology has implications for further MAP work. Each I-P-O theory generates predictions that can be checked against additional data; coherence across probes is a sanity check; sharp divergence is itself a finding. Further probes can be added if new I-O pairs surface (funder reports, REF outcomes, member-institution case studies, UKRI policy guidance). The MAP table is not closed at three probes — it is the current scattering state, with room to add probes that surface new angles.

### Surprises

- *T1 delivery overshot target by 4%.* The Programme delivered more T1 trainees than its internal target required (2,816 vs target ≈ 2,700, i.e., 104% over six full quarters). Neither WP1 nor the npt WP currently surface this as a finding. Worth flagging: the *delivery viability* dimension of the population model has empirical confirmation at network scale that the WP currently understates.
- *Per-institution variance is extreme.* Newcastle (482) delivers ~16× as much as Glasgow (15) and ~140× the median single-digit institutions. WP1 did not anticipate this distribution; the npt WP's population model coded 10 specific profiles but not the network's full delivery-count variance. The distribution itself is a candidate target for verification work.
- *WP1's "evaluation-and-landscape-review" third evidence layer has not yet been delivered as a public WP.* WP1 explicitly named it as the third sequential layer ("Evaluation and landscape review methods will be deployed within the Programme, to ensure continual improvement as the work proceeds"). Joe's draft, contributed via OBU and KCL, is a candidate for that third layer. This mission is, in part, *what WP1 anticipated would come next*.
- *WP09 is honest about indicators-track maturity.* Most automated indicators are not yet operational at sector scale; only pre-registration, FAIRness components, and CRediT detection are operational at *pilot* scale. This is direct evidence on the current operational status of the *Visible Impact* candidate pattern's gate factors (systematisation, communal-appraisal): they are partially constructible, not yet at scale. UKRN-S contractors looking for *demonstrable change* (the practical synonym for Visible Impact) get a partial answer at present, with named gaps and a named coordination need (OSMI).
- *The three tracks have not been integrated.* The training track (GENOTYPE: WP1, T1 counts, ORP T3 evaluation), the indicators track (EXOTYPE: WP02, WP09 pilots), and the sector-priorities track (XENOTYPE: 2022 Enhancing Research Culture, 2024 UUK blueprint) have run in parallel but have not been operationally coupled. UKRN-S inherits all three. Training without indicators leaves outcomes unverifiable; indicators without sector-frame integration produces measurement without policy traction; sector-frame without training is positioning without product. The mission's design has to address all three coupling problems, not just the training/indicators one named earlier.
- *Funder catalysis is named in the XENOTYPE pair but not yet in the npt WP.* The 2022 Enhancing Research Culture work explicitly says ringfenced funding (Research England's £28m for 2021–22) "has been instrumental for institutions to work on internal or small collaborative activities." This identifies the funding lever as a sector-level driver that the npt WP's tension framework does not currently surface. UKRN-S as a CIC has its own funding question; this is an external lever the framework should make legible.

## 3. DERIVE

### Frame: from triangulation to equippage

DERIVE takes MAP's three-track triangulation (training / indicators / sector — GENOTYPE / EXOTYPE / XENOTYPE) and produces a design that addresses the four sorries while staying coherent across all three angles. The aim is not commencement-address advice ("here are tensions; manage them well") but *equippage*: a setup the implementing team picks up and acts on. Each role has named strengths and limitations; coordination structures specify who owes what to whom; invariants are checkable; non-obvious decisions are inline-justified.

Anchored in futon-theory: `coordination-protocol` (multi-channel handoff with review gates), `proof-path` (each sorry needs a discharge route), `durability-first` (what you save is what you get back — relevant to fidelity contract), `agent-contract` (writing-team → implementing-team hand-off at VERIFY exit), and `structural-tension-as-observation` (tensions are signals, not stipulations).

### Roles and responsibilities

Five roles carry the design. The profiles are deliberately uneven — each role has named strengths AND limitations, and the coordination problem is the work the team does *together*.

#### 1. The Training Manager (Elle Lahart)

- *Strengths.* Operational delivery; programme management; relationship management with Institutional Leads; intimate knowledge of T3/T1 mechanics; the operational substrate.
- *Limitations.* Part-time bandwidth; cannot single-handedly carry analytical work; cannot do sector-level engagement at scale.
- *Needs from others.* Analytical work on T1 outcomes from the Evaluator; sector-level engagement from the Convener; theoretical and verification input from the Researcher-Advisor.
- *Uniquely supplies.* The operational substrate — training actually happens because of the Training Manager. Without this role, the GENOTYPE track is dormant.

#### 2. The Evaluator (the EV_analyse role; configurable across centralised, distributed, and coordinator variants)

The Evaluator role is not a fixed appointment but a configurable function. Per the Sorry-2 reframe (evaluation timing, not evaluator presence), the role's job is *continuous in-flight evaluation that feeds back into ongoing decisions*, not retrospective integration. UKRN-S can configure this function in three ways, and the AIF simulation tests which works best under what conditions:

- *Centralised configuration*: a single designated evaluator role (one person, part-time minimum) carries the analysis. Closes Sorry 2 quickly but adds a single-point dependency.
- *Distributed configuration*: peer evaluation across member institutions (Step 3 of the WP bootstrap). Closes Sorry 3 simultaneously but takes longer to stand up and depends on governance investment.
- *Coordinator configuration*: an evaluation coordinator pulls together an otherwise-distributed evaluation approach. Hybrid; potentially the highest yield but also the highest coordination overhead.

Common across all three configurations:

- *Strengths.* Cross-institutional analysis; pattern recognition across data; familiarity with NPT and the WP's pattern set; capacity to operate the population model and the cyber-ant simulation as verification tools.
- *Limitations.* Depends on data inputs from the Training Manager and Institutional Leads; cannot deliver training; not a sector-level operator.
- *Needs from others.* Data from delivery (T1 outcomes, Maria's forms, attendance counts); coordination access from the Convener (cross-institutional consent, governance); time allocation from the Training Manager.
- *Uniquely supplies.* The continuous-in-flight evaluation capability the ORP lacked. Without this role, Sorry 2 cannot close (evaluation defaults to retrospective), the indicators-track substrate (WP09 pilots) goes unused, and the *Visible Impact* candidate pattern lacks operational substrate.

Configuration choice is itself a DERIVE decision, deferred to ARGUE/VERIFY: the simulation tests trajectories under each configuration variant and produces evidence on which to commit to.

#### 3. The Convener (Neil Jacobs as chair, with services lead — confirmation of name pending)

- *Strengths.* Governance; sector-level relationships; OSMI / UUK / funder engagement; legitimacy across the UK research-culture frame.
- *Limitations.* Not operational in the day-to-day delivery sense; not analytical; lower bandwidth for granular work; carries political-rhetorical force that has to be balanced against the empirical discipline.
- *Needs from others.* Operational data from the Training Manager; analytical findings from the Evaluator; theoretical legibility from the Researcher-Advisor (so the offer can be articulated at sector level without overclaiming).
- *Uniquely supplies.* The XENOTYPE legibility — UKRN-S's commercial proposition is heard at sector level because of the Convener. Without this role, the XENOTYPE track is dormant; the UUK chapter-8 conversation passes UKRN-S by; the OSMI engagement does not happen.

#### 4. The Researcher-Advisor (Joseph Corneli; external; OBU)

- *Strengths.* Theoretical framing; verification; pattern induction; writing-coherence and system-coherence pattern libraries; cross-checking against simulation and against the WP's candidate framings.
- *Limitations.* External — no operational authority within UKRN-S; bandwidth is consultancy-style, not embedded; cannot sustain ongoing operational work.
- *Needs from others.* Data access from the Training Manager and Evaluator; framing decisions from the Convener (what to commit to); operational pull from the implementing team to keep the work load-bearing rather than ornamental.
- *Uniquely supplies.* Verification of the candidate framings (this mission's contribution); the writing-coherence and system-coherence pattern libraries; the briefing UKRN-S works from.

#### 5. The Institutional Leads (across 24+ institutions)

- *Strengths.* Local context; tactical knowledge of their institution; first-line delivery of T1 training; the relationships the network actually rests on; the substrate the T1 delivery counts measure.
- *Limitations.* Each Lead has only their institution's view; cross-institutional comparison is not their job; the role is uneven across institutions (Newcastle 482 trainees vs Glasgow 15 — a ~30× spread in active engagement); some Leads are dormant or post-handover.
- *Needs from others.* Scaffolds and frameworks from the Training Manager; analytical support from the Evaluator (what's worked elsewhere they should learn from); sector-level legitimisation from the Convener (what their institutional engagement maps to in the wider frame).
- *Uniquely supplies.* The tactical participation in evaluation that closes Sorry 3; the local-context evidence that makes the design responsive to actual institutional needs (and not just to the model's coded profiles).

### Coordination structures

The five roles connect through standing channels with named decision rights. Per `coordination-protocol`: explicit multi-channel handoff with review gates.

#### Standing channels

- *TM ↔ IL* (Training Manager ↔ Institutional Leads): existing institutional-lead network; quarterly convening; data flow on T1 delivery counts. Already operational.
- *TM ↔ EV* (Training Manager ↔ Evaluator): new; data handoff (T1 outcomes, Maria's forms, attendance); standing meeting before each T1 wave's analysis.
- *EV ↔ Convener* (Evaluator ↔ Convener): new; cross-institutional access (consent, governance, OSMI/sector engagement); analytical findings briefed to Convener for sector-level translation.
- *Convener ↔ External* (OSMI, UUK, funders): partial; sector-level engagement; Convener carries the relationships.
- *Researcher-Advisor ↔ all*: cross-cutting; primarily through mission and briefing documents; quarterly review of verification-track work.

#### Decision rights

- Operational decisions (curriculum, scheduling, T3 onboarding, T1 timing): Training Manager.
- Analytical scope (which T1 wave, which questions, which population-model extension): Evaluator, with Training Manager input.
- Sector-level commitments (OSMI engagement, UUK alignment, funder positioning): Convener.
- Theoretical and framing decisions (which candidate framings are committed to vs deferred): collaborative; Researcher-Advisor advises; Convener and Training Manager hold the decision.
- Local delivery: Institutional Leads, within scaffolds set by the Training Manager.

#### Review gates

- *Before T1 wave runs*: Evaluator + Training Manager review wave protocol; Convener notified for governance; PSR (if applicable) recorded.
- *Before sector-level commitment*: Convener + Researcher-Advisor cross-check the empirical basis; commitment recorded with named evidence.
- *Before service change in response to evidence*: Evaluator + Training Manager review the evidence trace (per *Treat design as evidence* pattern from the WP); change traces to the specific evidence update.

### Invariant principles

Checkable propositions that must hold for the design to be operational. Each is a sentence whose truth-value can be verified at any time.

1. *Every commissioned course has a named role-owner with allocated time.* (Avoids the *Goodwill-absorbed* mode the WP modes section names.)
2. *Every T1 evaluation is analysed by the Evaluator before its results inform service decisions.* (Avoids the *Reassurance machine* mode.)
3. *Every sector-level commitment is made by the Convener with documented evidence basis.* (Avoids floating-formalism at the policy frame.)
4. *Every design change traces to a specific evidence update; every non-change traces to absence of qualifying update.* (Per WP *Treat design as evidence* pattern.)
5. *Every Institutional Lead knows what their tactical participation in evaluation looks like and can carry it without consultation each time.* (Closes Sorry 3 operationally.)
6. *No role's bandwidth is over-subscribed without explicit reallocation.* (Avoids the *Apex-becomes-node* mode and the systemic absorbed-mode failure.)
7. *Each candidate framing carried from the WP has a current verification verdict on file (robust / robust-under-conditions / revised / set aside).* (Closes Sorry 4 incrementally.)

#### Predictions to test (one per DERIVE decision)

##### Prediction 1: Continuous in-flight evaluation is what closes Sorry 2 (timing, not presence)

- *Hypothesis.* The binding constraint is not whether evaluation happens but *when*. Continuous in-flight evaluation — feeding back into ongoing decisions — produces A rising with D. Retrospective evaluation (analysis at the end) produces A stalled even at high D, because the feedback never reaches the decisions.
- *Test.* Run two simulation configurations: (a) continuous-evaluation (Evaluator pattern triggers fire each tick that population state shifts), (b) retrospective-evaluation (Evaluator pattern triggers gate to fire only at terminal ticks).
- *Expected.* Configuration (a) shows A rising with D throughout the run; (b) shows A stalled mid-run with a late-stage step at the terminal evaluation moment — the *Reach-without-rigour* / *Data-rich-sense-poor* signature from the WP modes section.
- *Falsifier.* If (b) shows A climbing in step with D throughout, then evaluation timing is not the binding constraint — the Sorry-2 reframe is wrong and the original "evaluator absence" framing recovers.

##### Prediction 2: MVSG is conditional

- *Hypothesis.* Trajectories with MVSG-as-attractor baked into priors converge similarly to trajectories with role-coordination-only priors when both run under DERIVE's role-and-coordination structure.
- *Test.* Run two simulation configurations under the disciplined cyber-ant: (a) priors at the MVSG attractor (D = A = 0.85; the WP framing); (b) priors at the role-coordination centroid (computed from DERIVE's five-role structure without architectural framing).
- *Expected.* Final-state aggregates within ~5% across the two configurations. Trajectories may differ in shape but reach the multiplied region under both.
- *Falsifier.* If (b) significantly underperforms (a), the design depends on the MVSG framing more than DERIVE claimed; Decision 2's "robust to MVSG verdict" claim is wrong.

##### Prediction 3: Three coupling problems are explicit responsibilities

- *Hypothesis.* Coupled-roles configuration (TM ↔ EV; EV ↔ Convener; Convener ↔ TM all active) produces converging trajectories. Decoupled configuration (one or more couplings off) produces middling-mode failures matching the WP §5 modes section's named failures (Reach-without-rigour, Data-rich-sense-poor, Goodwill-absorbed).
- *Test.* Run four simulation configurations: (a) all three couplings active; (b) TM↔EV off (training without indicators); (c) EV↔Convener off (indicators without sector-frame); (d) Convener↔TM off (sector-frame without training).
- *Expected.* (a) converges; (b) shows Data-rich-sense-poor signature (high D, weak A); (c) shows Reach-without-rigour signature (high R, weak L); (d) shows Strategy-without-ground signature (high R, low L on training).
- *Falsifier.* If decoupled configurations also converge cleanly, coupling is not as load-bearing as DERIVE claims; the explicit responsibilities are over-specified.

##### Prediction 4: Funder catalysis as binding constraint

- *Hypothesis.* Trajectories with the funder-driver tension (t7) active converge faster (higher D progression rate) but with higher per-institution variance. Trajectories without t7 converge more slowly but more uniformly.
- *Test.* Run two simulation configurations: (a) funder-driver tension t7 active, calibrated from Research England £28m + extrapolation; (b) funder-driver tension t7 off (no funder pull).
- *Expected.* (a) reaches the multiplied region in fewer ticks but with higher inter-institution variance (some leap, others lag); (b) takes more ticks but produces tighter inter-institution variance. The trade-off itself is the substantive finding — *speed vs uniformity* — and is operationally relevant to UKRN-S funder-engagement strategy.
- *Falsifier.* If funder-driver tension does not change trajectory shape, Decision 4 is wrong and the funder-catalysis dynamic is not actually binding.

##### Prediction 5: Evaluator-role configuration matters

- *Hypothesis.* The three Evaluator-role configurations (centralised; distributed; coordinator) produce trajectories with different shapes and different yields, and the optimal configuration depends on network state — specifically on per-institution variance and on the share of institutions willing/able to do peer evaluation.
- *Test.* Run three simulation configurations under the disciplined cyber-ant: (a) centralised (one Evaluator agent firing pattern triggers); (b) distributed (Evaluator-pattern triggers distributed across Institutional Lead agents, with a fraction `p` of institutions willing/able to participate); (c) coordinator (one Evaluator-coordinator agent firing meta-triggers; institutional agents fire local-evaluation triggers).
- *Expected.* (a) closes Sorry 2 quickly but with a single-point-of-failure mode; (b) takes longer to stand up (governance overhead), produces broader A coordinate progress, but is brittle for low `p`; (c) higher coordination overhead but higher yield once running. The optimal varies with the empirical `p` value derived from MAP Q3 (which institutions are willing/able to participate tactically).
- *Falsifier.* If all three configurations produce indistinguishable trajectories, configuration choice is not load-bearing and DERIVE's Evaluator-role section can be simplified.
